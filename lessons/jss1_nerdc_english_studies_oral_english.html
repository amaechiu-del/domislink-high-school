Oral English: Stress and Intonation in Nigerian English

What is Stress and Intonation?

  • Stress means saying a syllable or word louder, longer, and higher in pitch.
  • Intonation is the rise and fall of your voice when you speak.
  • In English, stress can change the meaning of a word (e.g., reCORD (verb) vs REcord (noun)).
  • Falling intonation (↘) is used for statements and commands.
  • Rising intonation (↗) is used for yes/no questions.

Nigerian Examples

Example 1: In Nigeria, we often say “I am COMing” with stress on the first syllable. But in standard English, the stress is on the second syllable: “I am comING.”

Example 2: The word “TElephone” (stress on first syllable) is common in Nigerian English. Standard English stresses the first syllable too, but the vowel sounds differ: “TELephone.”

🎉 Fun Fact

Did you know? Nigerian English often uses a syllable-timed rhythm (each syllable gets equal time), while British English uses a stress-timed rhythm (stressed syllables come at regular intervals). That’s why our English sounds musical and unique!

Practice Questions

  1. Where is the stress in the word “CONduct” (noun) vs “conDUCT” (verb)?
    Answer: “CONduct” (noun) has stress on the first syllable; “conDUCT” (verb) has stress on the second.
  2. Which intonation would you use for the question: “Are you coming?”
    Answer: Rising intonation (↗) because it is a yes/no question.
  3. Say this sentence aloud: “She bought a PRESent for her friend.” Which word is stressed?
    Answer: “PRESent” (the noun) is stressed on the first syllable.
