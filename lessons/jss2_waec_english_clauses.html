Clauses in English

A clause is a group of words that contains a subject and a predicate. It is a fundamental unit of sentence structure in English.

There are two main types of clauses:

  • Independent Clause: A clause that has a subject and a predicate, and can stand alone as a complete sentence.
  • Dependent Clause: A clause that has a subject and a predicate, but cannot stand alone as a complete sentence.

Examples of clauses in Nigerian context:

  • "When I visited Lagos" is a dependent clause because it cannot stand alone as a complete sentence. For example, "I went to the market when I visited Lagos" is a complete sentence.
  • "Because I love jollof rice" is a dependent clause because it cannot stand alone as a complete sentence. For example, "I attended the party because I love jollof rice" is a complete sentence.

Fun Fact: The word "clause" comes from the Latin word "clausa", which means "a closing" or "a conclusion". This refers to the fact that a clause is a self-contained unit of meaning that can be closed off from other clauses.

Practice Questions:

  1. Identify the type of clause: "If I win the lottery"
    Answer: Dependent Clause
  2. Identify the type of clause: "I will attend the wedding"
    Answer: Independent Clause
  3. Combine the two clauses to form a complete sentence: "When I finish my homework" and "I will watch TV"
    Answer: When I finish my homework, I will watch TV
