Chemical Reactions: Transforming Matter

Key Concepts

  • Chemical Reaction: A process where substances (reactants) change into new substances (products) with different properties.
  • Reactants: The starting materials that combine or break apart during a reaction.
  • Products: The new substances formed as a result of the reaction.
  • Energy Changes: Most reactions either release heat (exothermic) or absorb heat (endothermic).
  • Indicators of a Reaction: Colour change, gas production (bubbles), temperature change, or formation of a precipitate.
  • Conservation of Mass: Atoms are rearranged—no atoms are created or destroyed in a chemical reaction.

🇳🇬 Nigerian Examples

Example 1: Burning of Charcoal (Yan Kwalli)
In many Nigerian homes, charcoal is burned for cooking. The carbon in charcoal reacts with oxygen in the air:
C + O₂ → CO₂
This is a combustion reaction that releases heat energy for cooking suya or boiling beans.

Example 2: Rusting of Iron Roofs (Zinc)
During the rainy season in Nigeria, iron sheets on roofs rust when exposed to water and oxygen:
4Fe + 3O₂ + 6H₂O → 4Fe(OH)₃
This is why many old buildings in Lagos or Ibadan have reddish-brown roofs.

🎉 Fun Fact

Did you know? The chemical reaction that makes puff-puff rise is the same one that makes bread fluffy! Yeast produces carbon dioxide (CO₂) gas through fermentation. The gas bubbles get trapped in the dough, making it expand. When you fry puff-puff, the heat causes more gas to expand, giving it that soft, spongy texture!

📝 Practice Questions

Q1: In the reaction 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O, identify the reactants and the product.

Show Answer

Answer: Reactants are hydrogen (H₂) and oxygen (O₂). The product is water (H₂O).

Q2: A student mixes baking soda and vinegar and sees bubbles forming. What does the formation of bubbles indicate?

Show Answer

Answer: The bubbles indicate that a gas (carbon dioxide, CO₂) is being produced, which is evidence of a chemical reaction.

Q3: When a log of wood burns in a fire, the total mass of the ashes is less than the original log. Does this violate the law of conservation of mass? Explain.

Show Answer

Answer: No, it does not violate the law. The missing mass was released as gases (carbon dioxide and water vapour) into the air. If you could collect all the gases and ashes, their total mass would equal the original mass of the wood plus the oxygen used in burning.
