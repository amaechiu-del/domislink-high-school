Key Concepts

  • Electric Current: The flow of electrons through a conductor (like copper wire). Measured in amperes (A).
  • Circuit: A closed loop that allows current to flow from the battery's positive terminal, through components, and back to the negative terminal.
  • Switch: Controls the circuit — when ON, current flows; when OFF, the circuit is broken.
  • Bulb: Converts electrical energy into light and heat energy.
  • Battery: Provides the voltage (push) that makes electrons move. Voltage is measured in volts (V).

Nigerian Examples

  1. PHCN Prepaid Meter: In many Nigerian homes, the prepaid meter uses an electric circuit to measure how much current your household uses. When you buy electricity units, the circuit allows current to flow; when units run out, the circuit is broken (like a switch turning off).
  2. NEPA Generator Change-over Switch: During power outages (often called "NEPA" in Nigeria), many homes use a generator. The change-over switch is a special circuit that safely disconnects the house from the PHCN grid and connects to the generator, preventing dangerous back-feeding.
⚡ Fun Fact: The first electric power plant in Nigeria was built in 1896 in Lagos, just 14 years after Thomas Edison built the world's first power plant in New York! It was used to power the Lagos Colony's streetlights.

Practice Questions

  1. What is the function of a switch in an electric circuit?
    Answer: A switch controls the flow of current — it can complete (ON) or break (OFF) the circuit, allowing or stopping the flow of electrons.

  2. If a bulb in a circuit does not light up, list two possible reasons.
    Answer: (i) The switch is OFF, breaking the circuit. (ii) The bulb is faulty (blown filament) or the battery is dead (no voltage).

  3. In the animated diagram above, what path do the electrons follow from the battery back to the battery?
    Answer: Electrons leave the negative (−) terminal of the battery, travel through the wire to the bulb, then through the switch (when ON), continue through the wire around the loop, and return to the positive (+) terminal of the battery.
