What is Radioactivity?

Radioactivity is the spontaneous decay of an unstable atomic nucleus, releasing energy and particles. This process is also called radioactive decay. It happens naturally in some elements like uranium, thorium, and radium.

Key Points About Radioactivity:

  • Unstable atoms (radioisotopes) try to become stable by releasing excess energy.
  • Three main types of radiation are emitted: alpha (α), beta (β), and gamma (γ).
  • Alpha particles are heavy and can be stopped by paper or skin.
  • Beta particles are lighter and can pass through paper but are stopped by aluminium.
  • Gamma rays are high-energy electromagnetic waves that require thick lead or concrete to stop.
  • Radioactivity is random — we cannot predict when a specific atom will decay.
  • The half-life is the time taken for half the radioactive atoms in a sample to decay.

Nigerian Examples of Radioactivity:

  1. Jos Plateau Tin Mining: Tin mining in Plateau State has exposed radioactive thorium in the soil. Miners and local communities need to be aware of radiation safety measures to avoid long-term health effects.
  2. NNPC Oil and Gas Operations: During oil drilling in the Niger Delta, naturally occurring radioactive materials (NORM) like radium-226 are brought to the surface. The Nigerian Nuclear Regulatory Authority (NNRA) monitors this to protect workers and the environment.
⭐ Fun Fact: Did you know that bananas contain a tiny amount of radioactive potassium-40? You would need to eat about 10 million bananas at once to feel any effects of radiation! This is why scientists say "banana equivalent dose" to explain small radiation amounts.

Practice Questions:

  1. What are the three main types of radiation emitted during radioactive decay?
  2. Why is gamma radiation more dangerous to living cells than alpha radiation?
  3. If a radioactive sample has a half-life of 5 years, how much of a 100g sample will remain after 15 years?

Answers:

  1. Alpha (α), Beta (β), and Gamma (γ) radiation.
  2. Gamma radiation is more dangerous because it has very high penetrating power — it can pass through the body and damage internal organs and DNA, while alpha particles are stopped by skin or even a sheet of paper.
  3. After 15 years (three half-lives): 100g → 50g (after 5 years) → 25g (after 10 years) → 12.5g (after 15 years).
