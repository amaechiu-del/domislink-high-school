Balance in Nature

Basic Science – JSS3 (WAEC Syllabus)

Key Points

  • Balance in Nature means that all living and non-living things interact in a stable, harmonious way.
  • Each organism has a role: plants produce oxygen, animals eat plants or other animals, decomposers recycle nutrients.
  • Food chains and webs show how energy flows from one organism to another.
  • If one part of the chain is removed or added, it can upset the balance (e.g., too many hunters → fewer prey → plant overgrowth).
  • Human activities like deforestation, pollution, and overfishing can break the natural balance.
  • Conservation efforts help restore and maintain the balance for future generations.

🇳🇬 Nigerian Examples

Example 1: The Niger Delta Mangroves
Mangrove forests in the Niger Delta provide breeding grounds for fish and protect the coast from erosion. When oil spills occur, the mangroves die → fish disappear → fishermen lose their livelihood → the entire ecosystem is thrown off balance.

Example 2: The Obudu Plateau (Cross River State)
The Obudu Plateau is home to unique plants and animals. When farmers clear large areas for farming (deforestation), soil erosion increases, streams dry up, and animals like the drill monkey lose their habitat. This disrupts the natural balance of the plateau.

🌟 Fun Fact: In Nigeria, the Iroko tree is considered sacred in many cultures. Cutting it down without permission is believed to bring bad luck – but scientifically, removing big trees can collapse the local food web and cause soil erosion! Nature and tradition often agree.

📝 Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: What does "Balance in Nature" mean?
    Answer: It means that all living things (plants, animals, humans) and non-living things (air, water, soil) interact in a stable and sustainable way, where no species dominates or destroys the others.
  2. Question 2: Give one way human activity can upset the balance in a Nigerian forest.
    Answer: Cutting down trees (deforestation) for farming or timber removes animal habitats, reduces oxygen, and causes soil erosion – this breaks the natural balance.
  3. Question 3: Why are fish important in the balance of nature in the Niger Delta?
    Answer: Fish eat small aquatic organisms and are eaten by larger animals (like birds and humans). They help control insect populations and recycle nutrients in the water. Without fish, the entire food web collapses.
