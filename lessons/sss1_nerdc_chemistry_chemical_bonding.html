Chemical Bonding: The Glue of Matter

Key Concepts of Chemical Bonding

  • Chemical bond = the force that holds atoms together in a compound.
  • Covalent bonding = atoms share electrons to achieve a full outer shell (e.g., H₂O, O₂).
  • Ionic bonding = transfer of electrons from a metal to a non-metal, forming oppositely charged ions (e.g., NaCl).
  • Metallic bonding = a "sea of delocalized electrons" holds positive metal ions together.
  • Atoms bond to become more stable (like noble gases with 8 valence electrons — the octet rule).

🇳🇬 Nigerian Examples

1. Table salt (NaCl) from the Niger Delta salt pans: Sodium (metal) transfers an electron to chlorine (non-metal), forming Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions — an ionic bond. This is why salt dissolves in water and conducts electricity when molten.

2. Cooking gas (LPG — propane/butane): Carbon and hydrogen atoms share electrons in covalent bonds. This is why gas burns easily — the bonds break and release energy used for cooking in Nigerian homes.

💡 Fun Fact

Did you know that the diamond found in some Nigerian states (like Kaduna) is pure carbon held together by strong covalent bonds in a giant network? That’s why diamond is the hardest natural substance on Earth! Meanwhile, graphite (also pure carbon) is soft because its layers are held by weak forces — that's why pencil lead writes on paper!

📝 Practice Questions

Q1. What type of bond is formed when two chlorine atoms combine to form a chlorine molecule (Cl₂)?

Show Answer

Answer: A covalent bond (specifically a single covalent bond) — each chlorine atom shares one electron to complete its octet.

Q2. In the formation of sodium chloride (NaCl), which atom loses an electron and which atom gains an electron?

Show Answer

Answer: Sodium (Na) loses one electron to become Na⁺ (positive ion). Chlorine (Cl) gains that electron to become Cl⁻ (negative ion). The opposite charges attract — this is an ionic bond.

Q3. Why does copper metal conduct electricity, but copper(II) oxide (CuO) does not?

Show Answer

Answer: Copper metal has metallic bonding with a "sea of delocalized electrons" that can move freely and carry electric current. In CuO, the copper and oxygen are held by ionic bonds — electrons are tightly held, so no free electrons exist to conduct electricity.
