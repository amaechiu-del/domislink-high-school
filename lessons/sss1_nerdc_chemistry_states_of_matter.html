States of Matter

Chemistry | SSS1 (NERDC Syllabus)

Key Points

  • Solid: Particles are tightly packed in a fixed arrangement. They vibrate in place but do not move freely. Solids have a definite shape and volume.
  • Liquid: Particles are close together but can slide past one another. Liquids have a definite volume but take the shape of their container.
  • Gas: Particles are far apart and move rapidly in all directions. Gases have no fixed shape or volume — they expand to fill any container.
  • Changes between states (e.g., melting, boiling, condensation) involve the gain or loss of heat energy.

Nigerian Examples

  1. Solid: Gari (cassava flakes) — a common staple food. Its particles stay in a fixed shape unless ground or mixed with water.
  2. Liquid: Palm oil — used in many Nigerian soups. It flows to take the shape of the pot or bottle it is stored in.

💡 Fun Fact: In Nigeria, pure water (sachet water) is a liquid at room temperature, but if you put it in a freezer, it becomes a solid — ice! This is a physical change called freezing.

Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: Which state of matter has particles that are far apart and move freely?
    Answer: Gas.
  2. Question 2: Name the process by which a liquid changes into a gas. Give a Nigerian example.
    Answer: Evaporation (or boiling). Example: Water in a pot on a kerosene stove turning into steam.
  3. Question 3: Why does a block of agbado (maize/corn) keep its shape, but zobo drink does not?
    Answer: Agbado is a solid with fixed particle arrangement, so it keeps its shape. Zobo is a liquid; its particles flow and take the shape of the container.
