Understanding Demand & Supply

  • Demand = How much consumers want to buy at different prices. The demand curve slopes downward — as price falls, quantity demanded rises.
  • Supply = How much producers are willing to sell at different prices. The supply curve slopes upward — as price rises, quantity supplied rises.
  • Equilibrium (E) = The point where demand meets supply. At price P* and quantity Q*, the market is stable.
  • Shortage occurs when price is below equilibrium — demand exceeds supply.
  • Surplus occurs when price is above equilibrium — supply exceeds demand.
  • Market forces naturally push price toward equilibrium (the "invisible hand").

Nigeria Examples

Example 1: Garri in the Wet Season
During the rainy season, cassava harvests increase → supply of garri rises. The supply curve shifts right, causing equilibrium price to drop. Consumers buy more garri at lower prices.
Example 2: Fuel Price Hike (Subsidy Removal)
When the Nigerian government reduced fuel subsidies, the price of petrol increased sharply. At the new higher price, quantity demanded fell (movement along demand curve), while supply remained relatively fixed in the short term.
🎉 Fun Fact!
In Nigeria, the price of pure water (sachet water) often rises during harmattan season because supply drops (less water production) while demand stays high. This is a real-world example of supply shifting left!

Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: If the price of beans in a Lagos market is ₦800 per cup, and at this price, sellers want to sell 500 cups but buyers only want 300 cups, what is this situation called? What should happen to the price?
  2. Question 2: During the Christmas season in Nigeria, demand for chicken increases. Using the demand-supply diagram, explain what happens to the equilibrium price and quantity of chicken.
  3. Question 3: A new technology helps Nigerian rice farmers produce more rice per hectare. Which curve shifts? What happens to the equilibrium price of local rice?
Answers:
1. Surplus (excess supply). Price will fall toward equilibrium as sellers compete to sell their beans.
2. The demand curve shifts right (increase in demand). Both equilibrium price and equilibrium quantity rise.
3. The supply curve shifts right (increase in supply). Equilibrium price of local rice falls, while equilibrium quantity rises.
