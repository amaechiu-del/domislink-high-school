What is Comprehension?

Comprehension is the ability to read a passage, understand its meaning, and interpret the author's message. It involves:

  • Literal meaning: Understanding facts and details directly stated in the text.
  • Inferential meaning: Reading between the lines to grasp implied ideas.
  • Vocabulary in context: Using surrounding words to figure out unfamiliar terms.
  • Main idea & supporting details: Identifying the central point and how it is developed.
  • Author's purpose: Recognizing whether the writer aims to inform, persuade, entertain, or explain.

🇳🇬 Nigerian Examples

Example 1: "The harmattan morning was dry and dusty. Kola pulled his agbada closer as he walked to the motor park."
Comprehension question: Why did Kola pull his agbada closer? Answer: Because the harmattan morning was cold and dusty.

Example 2: "After waiting for two hours under the mango tree, Ada finally saw the yellow bus coming from Umuahia."
Comprehension question: What was Ada doing under the mango tree? Answer: She was waiting for a bus from Umuahia.

💡 Fun Fact

Did you know? The word "comprehension" comes from Latin comprehendere, meaning "to grasp together." The human brain can process up to 400 words per minute when reading — but comprehension slows down to about 200–300 words per minute for deep understanding!

📝 Practice Questions

Read the short passage below, then answer the questions.

"Chidi’s father bought a new generator last week because the electricity in their Lagos neighbourhood had become very unreliable. On Friday evening, while Chidi was doing his homework by candlelight, the generator suddenly coughed and stopped. 'NEPA has taken light again,' his mother sighed."

  1. Why did Chidi’s father buy a new generator?
  2. What was Chidi doing when the generator stopped?
  3. What does the phrase "NEPA has taken light again" mean in this context?

✅ Answers

  1. Because the electricity in their neighbourhood was very unreliable.
  2. He was doing his homework by candlelight.
  3. It means the power supply (electricity) has gone off again — a common expression in Nigeria referring to the national power grid failure.
