Lexis: Expanding Your Vocabulary

  • Lexis refers to the complete set of words in a language (its vocabulary).
  • In English, lexis includes single words (e.g., house) and multi-word units (e.g., kick the bucket meaning "die").
  • Building your lexis means learning new words, their meanings, and how to use them correctly in context.
  • Lexical items can be categorized as: content words (nouns, verbs, adjectives) and function words (prepositions, conjunctions).
  • Idioms, proverbs, and collocations are all part of a language's lexis.

🇳🇬 Nigerian Examples

1. "The man is a big grammar." — Here, "big grammar" (a Nigerian English lexical item) means someone who is educated or speaks eloquently, not literally large grammar.

2. "She carried her load to the village." — In Nigeria, "carry load" can mean to move one's belongings, but in standard lexis it means to transport a burden. Context determines meaning.

💡
Fun Fact: The English language has over 170,000 words in current use, but the average Nigerian SSS1 student uses only about 5,000–7,000 words actively. Learning just 10 new words per week can double your active lexis in one year!

Practice Questions

  1. What is the difference between lexis and grammar?
  2. Identify the lexical item in this Nigerian sentence: "My friend is a been-to." What does it mean?
  3. Give one example of a collocation (words that often go together) and explain its meaning.

1. Lexis refers to vocabulary (words and phrases), while grammar is the set of rules for combining those words into sentences.

2. The lexical item is "been-to" — it means a person who has travelled abroad (often to the UK or US) and returned to Nigeria.

3. Example: "make a decision" (collocation of "make" + "decision"). It means to choose or decide something. Other examples: "strong coffee," "commit a crime."
