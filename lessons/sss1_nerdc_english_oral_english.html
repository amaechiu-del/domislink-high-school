Key Points about Oral English

  • Oral English focuses on the correct production of speech sounds using the organs of speech (lips, tongue, teeth, palate, etc.).
  • Proper articulation ensures clarity—each sound is formed by precise movements of these organs.
  • Voiced vs. unvoiced sounds: For example, /b/ (voiced) uses vocal cord vibration, while /p/ (unvoiced) does not.
  • Intonation and stress affect meaning. Rising intonation often signals a question, while falling intonation signals a statement.
  • Regular practice with minimal pairs (e.g., ship vs. sheep) helps distinguish similar sounds.

Nigerian Examples

  • Example 1: A student from Lagos says "I am going to the market" – ensure the /r/ sound in "market" is pronounced clearly, not dropped or replaced with /l/.
  • Example 2: An Igbo speaker practicing "She sells sea shells" – pay attention to the /ʃ/ (sh) sound vs. /s/ sound, which can be confused in some dialects.
Fun Fact! The English language has about 44 distinct sounds (phonemes), but only 26 letters. That's why one letter can represent different sounds—like the 'a' in cat, father, and cake!

Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: Which speech organ is primarily used to produce the /f/ sound (as in fish)?
    Answer: The lower lip touches the upper teeth (labiodental sound).
  2. Question 2: What is the difference between the /θ/ sound (as in think) and the /ð/ sound (as in this)?
    Answer: /θ/ is unvoiced (no vocal cord vibration), while /ð/ is voiced (vocal cords vibrate). Both are produced with the tongue tip between the upper and lower teeth.
  3. Question 3: In the word “present”, how does stress change the meaning?
    Answer: PREsent (noun = a gift) vs. preSENT (verb = to show or introduce). Stress shifts the word class.
