Understanding Constitutions

A constitution is the fundamental set of principles or established precedents according to which a state or other organization is governed. It defines the structure, powers, and limits of government, as well as the rights of citizens.

Key Features of a Constitution

  • Supremacy: The constitution is the highest law; all other laws and actions must conform to it.
  • Written or Unwritten: Constitutions can be codified (like Nigeria's) or based on customs and precedents (like the UK).
  • Separation of Powers: Divides government into Executive, Legislative, and Judicial branches to prevent abuse of power.
  • Protection of Fundamental Rights: Guarantees citizens' rights such as freedom of speech, religion, and fair trial.
  • Amendment Procedure: Provides a formal process for changing the constitution to adapt to new circumstances.

Nigerian Examples

Example 1: The 1999 Constitution of Nigeria
Nigeria operates under the 1999 Constitution (as amended). It establishes a federal system with 36 states and a Federal Capital Territory. It guarantees fundamental rights in Chapter IV, including the right to life, dignity, and freedom of expression. The constitution also provides for a presidential system with a clear separation of powers among the Executive (President), Legislature (National Assembly), and Judiciary (Courts).

Example 2: Constitutional Amendment for Local Government Autonomy
In 2023, the Nigerian National Assembly passed a constitutional amendment to grant financial and administrative autonomy to Local Government Councils. This change aimed to strengthen grassroots governance and reduce state government control over local areas. It required approval by at least 24 state assemblies before becoming law, demonstrating the amendment process.

💡 Fun Fact: Did you know that Nigeria's 1999 Constitution is one of the longest in the world? It contains 320 sections and 8 schedules! It was originally drafted by a military government and has been amended several times to reflect democratic changes.

Practice Questions

  1. What does the principle of "Separation of Powers" mean in a constitutional government?
  2. Name two fundamental rights guaranteed by the Nigerian Constitution.
  3. Why is the constitution considered the "supreme law" of a country?

Answers

  1. Separation of Powers means dividing government responsibilities into three distinct branches (Executive, Legislative, Judicial) to prevent any one branch from having too much power, ensuring checks and balances.
  2. Two fundamental rights are: (i) Right to life, and (ii) Right to freedom of expression. (Other correct answers: right to dignity, right to fair hearing, etc.)
  3. The constitution is the supreme law because all other laws, policies, and actions of the government must conform to it. Any law that contradicts the constitution is null and void, and the constitution provides the ultimate authority for governance.
