Understanding Prose in Literature (SSS1 – NERDC Syllabus)

What is Prose?

  • Prose is a form of written or spoken language that follows natural speech patterns, without the structured meter or rhyme of poetry.
  • It includes novels, short stories, novellas, and essays.
  • Key elements include: plot (sequence of events), character (people in the story), setting (time and place), and theme (central idea).
  • Prose can be fictional (imagined) or non-fictional (based on real events).

Nigerian Examples

Example 1: Things Fall Apart by Chinua Achebe – A classic Nigerian novel that explores pre-colonial Igbo life, the arrival of European missionaries, and the tragic fall of Okonkwo. It is a prose work rich in cultural setting and character development.

Example 2: The Joys of Motherhood by Buchi Emecheta – A novel that follows the life of Nnu Ego, a Nigerian woman navigating the challenges of motherhood, tradition, and urban life in Lagos. It is a powerful example of prose focusing on gender and societal themes.

🎉 Fun Fact

Did you know? The longest novel ever written in English is In Search of Lost Time by Marcel Proust, which contains over 1.2 million words! In contrast, many Nigerian prose works like The Passport of Mallam Ilia by Cyprian Ekwensi are short and action-packed, perfect for classroom study.

Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: Define prose and list two of its key elements.
  2. Question 2: Name one Nigerian prose work and state its main theme.
  3. Question 3: How does prose differ from poetry in terms of structure?

Answers

  1. Answer 1: Prose is written or spoken language in its ordinary form, without metrical structure. Two key elements are: plot (the sequence of events) and character (the individuals in the story).
  2. Answer 2: Things Fall Apart by Chinua Achebe – its main theme is the clash between traditional Igbo culture and colonial influence.
  3. Answer 3: Prose follows natural speech patterns and is arranged in sentences and paragraphs, while poetry uses meter, rhyme, and often line breaks for rhythm and effect.
