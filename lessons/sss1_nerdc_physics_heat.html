📌 Key Points About Heat

  • Heat is a form of energy that flows from a region of higher temperature to a region of lower temperature.
  • Temperature measures how hot or cold an object is, while heat is the energy transferred due to temperature difference.
  • Conduction is the transfer of heat through a material without the material itself moving — occurs in solids.
  • Good conductors (like metals) transfer heat quickly; poor conductors (like wood, plastic) are called insulators.
  • The rate of heat transfer depends on: temperature difference, material type, thickness, and surface area.

🇳🇬 Nigerian Examples

  • Example 1 — Making Akara: When frying akara (bean cakes) in palm oil, the heat from the fire conducts through the metal frying pan to cook the akara. The metal pan gets hot quickly because it is a good conductor of heat.
  • Example 2 — Cooling Garri: After roasting garri in a large metal pot, the hot garri is spread on a flat wooden tray to cool. Wood is a poor conductor (insulator), so it prevents the heat from escaping too fast, but the metal pot conducts heat away from the fire quickly during roasting.
🎉 Fun Fact! Did you know that the Sahara Desert in northern Nigeria can reach 50°C during the day, but because sand is a poor conductor, the heat stays near the surface — you can walk barefoot on sand at night and it feels cool!

📝 Practice Questions

  1. What is the main difference between heat and temperature?
  2. Why is the handle of a metal cooking pot often made of wood or plastic?
  3. If one end of a metal rod is placed in a fire, explain what happens to the other end after some time.
✅ Answers:
1. Heat is the energy transferred between objects due to temperature difference, while temperature is a measure of how hot or cold an object is (average kinetic energy of particles).
2. Wood and plastic are poor conductors of heat (insulators), so they prevent heat from traveling to the handle and burning the user’s hand.
3. Heat travels along the rod by conduction — the particles at the hot end vibrate faster and collide with neighboring particles, transferring energy until the far end also becomes hot.
