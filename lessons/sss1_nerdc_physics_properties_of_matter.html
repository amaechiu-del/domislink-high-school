Properties of Matter

Key Properties of Matter

  • Mass – The amount of matter in an object. Measured in kilograms (kg).
  • Volume – The space an object occupies. Measured in cubic metres (m³) or litres (L).
  • Density – Mass per unit volume (ρ = m/V). Determines whether an object floats or sinks.
  • States of Matter – Matter exists as solid, liquid, or gas depending on particle arrangement and energy.
  • Melting point & Boiling point – Specific temperatures at which a substance changes state.
  • Elasticity – Ability to return to original shape after deformation.
  • Brittleness – Tendency to break under stress without significant deformation.

Nigerian Examples

Example 1: Palm Oil (Density)
Fresh palm oil is less dense than water, so it floats on top when mixed with water during processing. This property is used in traditional palm oil extraction to separate oil from water.

Example 2: Clay Pots (Porosity & Brittleness)
Traditional clay pots from places like Abuja or Ilorin are porous and brittle. They allow water to seep through and cool the contents by evaporation, but they break easily if dropped due to low elasticity.

🌟 Fun Fact!
Did you know that the Baobab tree (Kuka in Hausa), found in northern Nigeria, can store up to 120,000 litres of water in its trunk? This is because its wood has a spongy structure that can absorb and hold large amounts of liquid – a unique property of matter!

Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: A block of wood has mass 400 g and volume 500 cm³. Calculate its density in g/cm³. Is it likely to float in water? (Density of water = 1 g/cm³)
  2. Question 2: Why does a metal spoon feel colder than a wooden spoon at the same room temperature?
  3. Question 3: If you heat a solid substance, what happens to the arrangement and motion of its particles?

Answers

Answer 1: Density = mass/volume = 400/500 = 0.8 g/cm³. Since 0.8 < 1, the block will float in water.

Answer 2: Metal is a better conductor of heat than wood. It quickly draws heat away from your hand, making it feel colder.

Answer 3: The particles gain kinetic energy, vibrate more vigorously, and move farther apart. If enough heat is added, the solid will melt into a liquid as the particles break free from their fixed positions.
