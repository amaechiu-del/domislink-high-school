Ecology: The Web of Life

Ecology is the study of how living organisms interact with each other and with their physical environment. It examines the relationships that sustain life on Earth.

Key Concepts in Ecology

  • Population: A group of organisms of the same species living in a specific area (e.g., a herd of cattle in a field).
  • Community: All the different populations that live and interact in a particular area (e.g., fish, water plants, and insects in a pond).
  • Ecosystem: A community of living organisms (biotic) interacting with their non-living (abiotic) environment (e.g., a forest including soil, water, and animals).
  • Food Chain: A linear sequence showing who eats whom (e.g., Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake).
  • Food Web: A more complex network of interconnected food chains in an ecosystem.

Nigerian Examples

  • Niger Delta Mangrove Ecosystem: Mangrove trees (producers) provide habitat for crabs and fish, which are eaten by birds like the African Fish Eagle. This ecosystem protects the coastline and supports local fishing communities.
  • Jos Plateau Grasslands: Grasses and shrubs support grazing animals like cattle and antelopes. Predators such as hyenas and jackals control herbivore populations, maintaining balance in the ecosystem.

Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: What is the difference between a population and a community in ecology?
  2. Question 2: In a food chain, what role do decomposers play? Give one example from a Nigerian ecosystem.
  3. Question 3: Why are mangrove forests important to the ecology of the Niger Delta?

Answers

  1. Answer 1: A population consists of individuals of the same species in an area (e.g., all Tilapia fish in a pond), while a community includes all different populations (e.g., Tilapia, water lilies, frogs) interacting in that area.
  2. Answer 2: Decomposers break down dead organisms and waste, returning nutrients to the soil. Example: Fungi and bacteria in the rainforests of Cross River State decompose fallen leaves.
  3. Answer 3: Mangrove forests prevent coastal erosion, provide breeding grounds for fish and crabs, and support local livelihoods. They are vital for biodiversity and climate regulation in the region.
