Reproduction in Living Organisms (SSS1 Biology)

Based on WAEC Syllabus

Key Concepts

  • Reproduction is the biological process by which new individuals (offspring) are produced from their parents.
  • Asexual reproduction involves only one parent and produces offspring that are genetically identical (clones). Examples include binary fission in Amoeba and budding in Hydra.
  • Sexual reproduction involves two parents, the fusion of male and female gametes (fertilisation), and produces genetically diverse offspring.
  • Fertilisation can be external (e.g., in fish and frogs) or internal (e.g., in humans and birds).
  • Pollination is the transfer of pollen grains from the anther to the stigma in flowering plants, a key step in plant sexual reproduction.

🇳🇬 Nigerian Examples

  • Yam (Dioscorea spp.) – Commonly propagated asexually using yam tubers. Farmers plant "yam setts" (small pieces of tuber) to produce new yam plants, ensuring the next harvest.
  • Maize (Zea mays) – Reproduces sexually via pollination. In northern Nigeria, maize plants rely on wind and insects to transfer pollen from tassels to silks, forming kernels.

🎉 Fun Fact!

Some lizards in Nigeria (like the Mabouya skink) can reproduce by parthenogenesis – a form of asexual reproduction where females produce offspring without any male! The young are all female clones of the mother.

✏️ Practice Questions

  1. Q1: Define reproduction and state two differences between asexual and sexual reproduction.
  2. Q2: Name one Nigerian crop that reproduces asexually and explain how it is propagated.
  3. Q3: What is pollination? Why is it important for maize production in Nigeria?

Answers

  • A1: Reproduction is the process of producing new offspring. Differences: Asexual involves one parent, no gametes, offspring are clones; Sexual involves two parents, gametes fuse, offspring are genetically varied.
  • A2: Yam – propagated by planting yam setts (small pieces of tuber) which grow into new plants.
  • A3: Pollination is the transfer of pollen from anther to stigma. For maize, it enables fertilisation and kernel formation, which is essential for grain yield.
