Key Points on National Income

  • National Income is the total monetary value of all final goods and services produced in a country within a given period (usually one year).
  • It measures the economic performance of a nation and helps compare living standards between countries.
  • The circular flow diagram shows how money moves between households, firms, government, and the financial sector.
  • There are three main approaches to measuring national income: Output (production), Income (earnings), and Expenditure (spending).
  • Adjustments are made for inflation to get real national income (nominal vs. real GDP).

🇳🇬 Nigerian Examples

Example 1: Oil & Gas Sector — In 2023, Nigeria's crude oil production contributed about 7.2% to the nation's GDP. When oil prices rise, national income increases, affecting government revenue and household spending.

Example 2: Agriculture & Tech Boom — Nigeria's agriculture sector (cassava, cocoa, yam) employs over 35% of the workforce. The rise of fintech (e.g., Flutterwave, Paystack) also adds billions to national income through services.

💡 Fun Fact

Did you know? Nigeria’s rebasing of its GDP in 2014 added 89% to the size of its economy overnight! This happened because new sectors (like telecoms, Nollywood, and banking) were counted for the first time — making Nigeria Africa’s largest economy.

📝 Practice Questions

  1. Define national income and list the three methods used to measure it.
  2. Why is the circular flow model important for understanding national income? Mention at least two sectors involved.
  3. Calculate: If a country’s total consumer spending is ₦500 billion, investment is ₦200 billion, government spending is ₦150 billion, and net exports are ₦50 billion, what is the national income (GDP) using the expenditure approach?

✅ Answers

  1. National income is the total value of all final goods and services produced in a country in a year. The three methods are: Output method, Income method, and Expenditure method.
  2. The circular flow model shows how money flows between households (consumers), firms (producers), government, and the financial sector. It helps economists track income, spending, and leakages (savings/taxes) or injections (investment/government spending).
  3. GDP = C + I + G + (X – M) = 500 + 200 + 150 + 50 = ₦900 billion.
