Comprehension Skills (SSS1 - WAEC Syllabus)

Key Comprehension Skills

  • Literal Comprehension: Understanding exactly what the text says (facts, dates, names).
  • Inferential Comprehension: Reading between the lines to deduce meaning not directly stated.
  • Vocabulary in Context: Using surrounding words to figure out the meaning of unfamiliar terms.
  • Main Idea vs. Supporting Details: Distinguish the central point from the evidence that backs it up.
  • Author's Purpose: Determine if the text aims to inform, persuade, entertain, or explain.

Nigerian Examples

Example 1 (Literal): "The harmattan season in Northern Nigeria often brings dusty winds and cooler mornings."
Question: What brings dusty winds? Answer: The harmattan season.

Example 2 (Inferential): "As the sun set over Lagos Lagoon, the fishermen pulled their nets, but their faces were long."
Question: How did the fishermen feel? Answer: Disappointed (implied by "faces were long").

Fun Fact: Did you know that the longest word in the English language (45 letters) is "pneumonoultramicroscopicsilicovolcanoconiosis"? But in WAEC comprehension, you'll never need to spell it — just understand it from context!

Practice Questions

Passage: "In many Nigerian villages, the town crier still plays a vital role. Beating a wooden gong, he announces community meetings or emergencies. Though modern phones exist, the crier ensures no one is left out, especially the elderly who may not own a phone."

  1. Question 1: What does the town crier use to announce messages?
    Answer: A wooden gong.
  2. Question 2: Why is the town crier still important despite modern phones?
    Answer: He ensures the elderly (who may not own phones) are not left out.
  3. Question 3: What does the word "vital" most likely mean in this context? (a) Optional (b) Unnecessary (c) Essential (d) Rare
    Answer: (c) Essential.
