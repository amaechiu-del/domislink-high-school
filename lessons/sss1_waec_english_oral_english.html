Key Points on Oral English (Phonetics)

  • Vowels – sounds produced without obstruction: /i:/ (seat), /u:/ (food), /ɔ:/ (port).
  • Consonants – sounds produced with partial or complete obstruction: /p/ (pat), /b/ (bat), /tʃ/ (church).
  • Diphthongs – gliding sounds like /ai/ (time), /ei/ (day), /ɔi/ (boy).
  • Stress – emphasis on a syllable: record (verb) vs record (noun).
  • Intonation – rise and fall of pitch; rising tone for yes/no questions, falling for statements.

Nigerian Examples

  1. Example 1 – Vowel Contrast: "Sheep" /ʃi:p/ vs "Ship" /ʃɪp/. In many Nigerian accents, these are often merged; practice distinguishing long vs short vowels.
  2. Example 2 – Consonant Cluster: "Strength" /strɛŋθ/. Many speakers omit the /θ/ sound, saying "stren" instead. Use the tongue between the teeth to produce the "th" sound.
🎉 Fun Fact: The English word "queue" is pronounced the same as the letter "Q" — but it has four silent letters! This is why Oral English drills are important: spelling doesn't always tell you the sound.

Practice Questions

  1. Q1: Which of the following words has a different vowel sound?
    a) heat   b) hit   c) seat   d) feet
    Answer: b) hit (short /ɪ/ vs long /i:/)
  2. Q2: Identify the stressed syllable in the word "photograph".
    Answer: PHOto·graph (first syllable)
  3. Q3: Which consonant sound is represented by the letters "th" in the word "the"?
    Answer: /ð/ (voiced dental fricative – tongue between teeth, voice on)
