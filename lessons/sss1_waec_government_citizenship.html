Citizenship (WAEC Government – SSS1)

Key Concepts

  • Citizenship is the legal bond between an individual and a state, conferring rights and duties.
  • Rights include: right to life, freedom of speech, right to vote, and access to justice.
  • Duties include: obeying laws, paying taxes, and defending the nation when required.
  • Citizenship by Birth – automatic for those born in Nigeria or to Nigerian parents (jus soli / jus sanguinis).
  • Citizenship by Registration – for non-Nigerians who meet legal requirements (e.g., marriage, residency).
  • Citizenship can be lost through renunciation, deprivation, or acquisition of another citizenship (in some cases).

🇳🇬 Nigerian Examples

  1. Example 1: A child born in Lagos to Igbo parents is a Nigerian citizen by birth. This child automatically enjoys all constitutional rights, such as the right to education and the right to vote at age 18.
  2. Example 2: A British woman married to a Nigerian man for 10 years can apply for Nigerian citizenship by registration. She must renounce her British citizenship and take an oath of allegiance.

💡 Fun Fact: Did you know? Nigeria’s 1999 Constitution (Section 25) grants citizenship to anyone born outside Nigeria whose grandparents were Nigerian citizens. That’s how many diaspora Nigerians remain connected to their homeland!

📝 Practice Questions

  1. Define citizenship and list two rights of a Nigerian citizen.
  2. Explain the difference between citizenship by birth and citizenship by registration in Nigeria.
  3. State three duties of a Nigerian citizen to the state.

✅ Answers

  1. Answer 1: Citizenship is the legal status linking a person to a state. Rights include: right to life and freedom of speech.
  2. Answer 2: Citizenship by birth is automatic for those born in Nigeria or to Nigerian parents. Citizenship by registration is a legal process for non-Nigerians meeting conditions (e.g., marriage, residency).
  3. Answer 3: Duties include: obeying the law, paying taxes, and defending Nigeria when called upon.
