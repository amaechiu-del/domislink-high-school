Lesson: The Nigerian Constitution (SSS1 Government - WAEC Syllabus)

Key Features of the Nigerian Constitution

  • Supremacy: The Constitution is the highest law; any other law inconsistent with it is void.
  • Federalism: Power is shared between the Federal, State, and Local governments.
  • Separation of Powers: Executive, Legislature, and Judiciary have distinct functions.
  • Fundamental Rights: Guarantees rights like life, dignity, fair hearing, and freedom of expression.
  • Amendment Process: Requires a two-thirds majority in both houses of the National Assembly and approval by at least two-thirds of state Houses of Assembly.

Nigerian Examples

Example 1: The 1999 Constitution
Nigeria’s current constitution (as amended) establishes a presidential system. It was enacted on May 29, 1999, marking the beginning of the Fourth Republic. It provides for a bicameral legislature (Senate and House of Representatives) and a directly elected President.

Example 2: The Case of Lakamni v. Attorney-General of the Federation (1970)
The Supreme Court declared that the Federal Military Government's decree (Decree No. 28 of 1970) was unconstitutional because it violated the right to fair hearing. This case affirmed the supremacy of the Constitution even over military decrees during the period of military rule.

⭐ Fun Fact: Nigeria's first constitution (the Clifford Constitution of 1922) introduced the elective principle for the first time, but only African males in Lagos and Calabar who earned at least £100 a year could vote! Today, the 1999 Constitution guarantees every citizen aged 18 and above the right to vote.

Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: What is the supreme law of Nigeria?
  2. Question 2: Name the three tiers of government in Nigeria as provided by the Constitution.
  3. Question 3: How can the Nigerian Constitution be amended?

Answers:

Answer 1: The Constitution of the Federal Republic of Nigeria (1999, as amended) is the supreme law.

Answer 2: Federal Government, State Government, and Local Government.

Answer 3: An amendment requires a two-thirds majority vote in both the Senate and the House of Representatives, followed by approval by at least two-thirds of the 36 state Houses of Assembly.
