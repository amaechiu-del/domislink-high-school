🔍 What Are Prescribed Texts?

  • Prescribed texts are novels, plays, and poems selected by WAEC for the Literature-in-English syllabus.
  • They are grouped into three genres: Drama, Prose, and Poetry.
  • Students must study at least one text from each genre for the examination.
  • Questions test your understanding of plot, characters, themes, language, and context.
  • Memorizing key quotes and understanding the author’s background is essential for high marks.

🇳🇬 Nigerian Examples

1. Drama: The Lion and the Jewel by Wole Soyinka – A play exploring tradition vs. modernity in a Yoruba village, with characters like Baroka (the Bale) and Lakunle (the westernized schoolteacher).

2. Prose: The Old Man and the Medal by Ferdinand Oyono – A satirical novel about colonial injustice in Cameroon (often studied alongside Nigerian works for its West African perspective).

💡 Fun Fact: WAEC rotates prescribed texts every few years! For example, Weep Not, Child by Ngũgĩ wa Thiong’o was replaced by The Old Man and the Medal in recent syllabi. Always check the latest WAEC syllabus for your year.

📝 Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: In The Lion and the Jewel, what does the character of Lakunle represent in the conflict between tradition and modernity?
    Answer: Lakunle represents the blind embrace of Western modernity and rejection of African tradition, shown through his obsession with books, his rejection of bride price, and his inability to win Sidi.

  2. Question 2: Identify two major themes in The Old Man and the Medal.
    Answer: (i) Colonial hypocrisy and exploitation – the medal is a symbol of empty honor. (ii) The clash between African dignity and European domination – Meka’s eventual disillusionment.

  3. Question 3: Why is it important to study the socio-political context of a prescribed text?
    Answer: Context helps explain characters’ motivations, themes, and the author’s message. For example, understanding colonial Nigeria helps interpret the satire in Soyinka’s works or the critique in Oyono’s novel.
