Measurement in Physics (SSS1 - WAEC Syllabus)

Key Concept: Measurement is the process of comparing an unknown quantity with a known standard (unit). Every measurement consists of a number and a unit.

📏 Key Points About Measurement

  • Fundamental quantities (base units): length (metre, m), mass (kilogram, kg), time (second, s), electric current (ampere, A), temperature (kelvin, K), amount of substance (mole, mol), luminous intensity (candela, cd).
  • Derived quantities are formed from fundamental ones (e.g., speed = m/s, area = m², volume = m³).
  • Precision depends on the instrument's smallest division (e.g., a ruler marked in mm gives 0.1 cm precision).
  • Accuracy is how close a measurement is to the true value; avoid parallax error by reading at eye level.
  • SI units (Système International) are the standard for scientific measurement worldwide.

🇳🇬 Nigerian Examples

Example 1: A trader in Onitsha market measures 5 metres of Ankara fabric using a tailor's tape. If the tape is stretched too tight, will the customer get more or less fabric? (Answer: Less — stretching makes each division longer, so fewer actual metres are measured.)

Example 2: A Physics student in Lagos uses a stopwatch to time a falling object. The stopwatch reads 2.45 s. If the student started the watch 0.2 s late, what is the actual time of fall? (Answer: 2.45 s + 0.2 s = 2.65 s)

⭐ Fun Fact: The original "metre" was defined in 1793 as one ten-millionth of the distance from the equator to the North Pole along a meridian through Paris. Today, it's defined using the speed of light — exactly 1/299,792,458 of a second!

✏️ Practice Questions (WAEC-style)

  1. Q1: Convert 2500 millimetres to metres.
    Answer: 2500 mm ÷ 1000 = 2.5 m
  2. Q2: A student measures the length of a table as 1.24 m using a metre rule. What is the absolute error if the true length is 1.30 m?
    Answer: Absolute error = |1.30 - 1.24| = 0.06 m
  3. Q3: Which of the following is NOT a fundamental quantity? (a) Mass (b) Time (c) Volume (d) Temperature
    Answer: (c) Volume — it is a derived quantity (length³)
