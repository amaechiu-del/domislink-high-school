Ecology: The Web of Life

Ecology is the study of how living organisms interact with each other and with their physical environment. It explores the relationships that sustain life on Earth, from tiny microbes to vast forests.

Key Concepts in Ecology

  • Ecosystem: A community of living organisms (biotic) interacting with their non-living (abiotic) environment, e.g., a pond, forest, or savanna.
  • Food Chain & Food Web: Shows the flow of energy from producers (plants) to consumers (herbivores, carnivores) and decomposers. A food web is more realistic, showing interconnected chains.
  • Trophic Levels: Feeding positions in a food chain: Producers (Level 1), Primary Consumers (Level 2), Secondary Consumers (Level 3), Tertiary Consumers (Level 4).
  • Energy Flow: Only about 10% of energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next; the rest is lost as heat or used for life processes.
  • Biogeochemical Cycles: Nutrients like carbon, nitrogen, and water cycle through ecosystems via processes like photosynthesis, respiration, and decomposition.

Nigerian Examples

Example 1: The Niger Delta Mangrove Ecosystem
Mangrove trees (producers) provide habitat for crabs, fish, and birds. The roots filter pollutants and protect the coastline. Local communities harvest fish and prawns, relying on this rich ecosystem for food and livelihood.

Example 2: The Jos Plateau Grasslands
Grasses and shrubs support grazing animals like cattle and antelopes. Predators such as pythons and hawks control herbivore populations. Overgrazing by cattle can lead to desertification, a major ecological challenge in the region.

🌟 Fun Fact: The African baobab tree (found in parts of northern Nigeria) can store up to 120,000 litres of water in its trunk! It acts as a natural reservoir, supporting many animals during the dry season—a perfect example of an ecological adaptation.

Practice Questions

  1. What is the primary source of energy for almost all ecosystems?
    Answer: The Sun.
  2. In a Nigerian savanna food chain (grass → grasscutter → python → hawk), which organism is the secondary consumer?
    Answer: The python (it eats the grasscutter, a primary consumer).
  3. Why do decomposers (like fungi and bacteria) play a vital role in an ecosystem?
    Answer: They break down dead organisms and waste, returning nutrients to the soil for producers to use again.
