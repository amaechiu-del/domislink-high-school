Key Points About Homeostasis

  • Definition: Homeostasis is the ability of the body to maintain a stable internal environment despite changes in the external environment.
  • Control Mechanism: It works through a negative feedback loop — when a condition deviates from normal, the body activates processes to reverse the change.
  • Components: Every homeostatic system has receptors (detect change), a control centre (usually the brain), and effectors (muscles or glands that make adjustments).
  • Examples of parameters: Body temperature (37°C), blood glucose (70–110 mg/dL), blood pH (7.35–7.45), and water balance.
  • Failure: When homeostasis breaks down, diseases occur — e.g., diabetes (blood sugar), dehydration (water balance), or hyperthermia (temperature).

Nigerian Examples

  • Example 1 — Harmattan Season: During the dry, dusty harmattan in Northern Nigeria, the air is cold and dry. The body responds by shivering (muscle contractions generate heat) and reducing blood flow to the skin (vasoconstriction) to conserve heat, keeping the core temperature at 37°C.
  • Example 2 — After Eating Garri: Garri (cassava flakes) is rich in carbohydrates. After a meal, blood glucose rises. The pancreas releases insulin, which helps cells absorb glucose and stores excess as glycogen in the liver, bringing blood sugar back to normal levels.
🌟 Fun Fact: Did you know that the human body can detect a change in blood temperature as small as 0.1°C? The hypothalamus in the brain acts like a thermostat, constantly monitoring and adjusting to keep you in the "Goldilocks zone" — not too hot, not too cold!

Practice Questions

  1. Define homeostasis and name two internal conditions that the body regulates.
    Answer: Homeostasis is the maintenance of a stable internal environment. Examples: body temperature and blood glucose level.
  2. Explain the role of the pancreas in regulating blood sugar after a Nigerian meal of pounded yam and egusi soup.
    Answer: After eating, blood glucose rises. The pancreas releases insulin, which helps cells take up glucose and stores excess as glycogen in the liver, reducing blood sugar to normal.
  3. During a hot afternoon in Lagos, you begin to sweat. Identify the receptor, control centre, and effector in this homeostatic response.
    Answer: Receptor = skin temperature sensors; Control centre = hypothalamus in the brain; Effector = sweat glands (produce sweat to cool the body by evaporation).
