Key Concepts in Chemical Kinetics

  • Reaction rate = change in concentration of reactant or product per unit time (mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹).
  • Collision theory: Particles must collide with sufficient energy (≥ activation energy, Ea) and correct orientation for a reaction to occur.
  • Factors affecting rate: Temperature, concentration, pressure (for gases), surface area, and presence of a catalyst.
  • Activation energy (Ea) is the minimum energy required for a reaction to proceed — shown as the energy barrier in the diagram above.
  • Catalysts provide an alternative reaction pathway with a lower Ea, speeding up the reaction without being consumed.

🇳🇬 Nigerian Examples

1. Ripening of plantain with calcium carbide: Many Nigerian fruit sellers use calcium carbide (CaC₂) to speed up the ripening of plantain and mangoes. The carbide reacts with moisture to produce acetylene gas, which acts as a catalyst to accelerate the natural ripening process — a practical application of kinetics in food processing.

2. Cooking of beans with potash (kanwa): In Nigerian homes, adding potash (potassium carbonate) to beans reduces cooking time. The potash raises the pH and lowers the activation energy for breaking down complex carbohydrates and proteins, demonstrating how catalysts (and temperature) affect reaction rates in everyday cooking.

💡 Fun Fact

The Haber process (used to make ammonia for fertilizers in Nigeria's agricultural sector) operates at about 450°C and 200 atm. Without a finely divided iron catalyst, the reaction would be so slow it would take years to produce usable amounts — but with the catalyst, it happens in minutes! This shows how catalysts make industrial chemistry possible.

Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: A reaction has an activation energy of 80 kJ mol⁻¹. At 300 K, the rate constant is 2.0 × 10⁻³ s⁻¹. If the temperature is raised to 310 K, the rate constant increases to 4.0 × 10⁻³ s⁻¹. Explain, using collision theory, why the rate increased even though the concentration remained constant.
  2. Question 2: In the production of "zobo" drink (from hibiscus leaves), why does crushing the dried hibiscus petals into smaller pieces before boiling extract the colour faster than using whole petals?
  3. Question 3: A student observes that magnesium ribbon reacts faster with dilute hydrochloric acid at 40°C than at 25°C. State two factors that could further increase the rate of this reaction, and explain how each factor works.

📝 Answers

Answer 1: Raising temperature increases the average kinetic energy of particles. More molecules now have energy ≥ Ea (80 kJ mol⁻¹), so a greater fraction of collisions are effective. Also, collision frequency increases slightly. Both factors increase the rate constant and reaction rate.

Answer 2: Crushing increases the surface area of the petals. More solid surface is exposed to the hot water, so more collisions occur between water molecules and the pigment molecules in the petals per unit time, speeding up extraction (a heterogeneous reaction).

Answer 3: Two factors: (i) Increase concentration of HCl — more acid particles per volume means more frequent collisions with magnesium. (ii) Use powdered magnesium instead of ribbon — larger surface area exposes more magnesium atoms to acid, increasing collision frequency. Both increase the rate of hydrogen gas production.
