Federalism: A System of Shared Power

Federalism is a system of government where power is constitutionally divided between a central (national) authority and regional (state) governments. Both levels operate independently in some areas but cooperate in others.

Key Features of Federalism

  • Two Levels of Government: A central (federal) government and several regional (state) governments.
  • Constitutional Division of Powers: The constitution clearly lists which powers belong to the federal government and which to the states.
  • Supremacy of the Constitution: The constitution is the highest law; any conflicting law is invalid.
  • Independent Judiciary: Courts settle disputes between the federal and state governments.
  • Bicameral Legislature: Often a two-chamber parliament (e.g., Senate and House of Representatives) representing both the people and the states.

Nigerian Examples

Example 1: Exclusive vs. Concurrent List
The Nigerian Constitution contains an Exclusive List (e.g., defence, currency, foreign affairs) only for the Federal Government, and a Concurrent List (e.g., education, health, agriculture) where both Federal and State governments can legislate. If a state law conflicts with a federal law on the Concurrent List, the federal law prevails.

Example 2: State Police vs. Federal Police
Nigeria currently operates a single national police force (Nigeria Police Force) under the Federal Government. Many states have called for State Police to handle local security issues like farmer-herder clashes, but the Federal Government has resisted, citing the need for national coordination. This debate shows the tension in Nigeria's federal system.

🌟 Fun Fact: Nigeria is one of the few countries in the world with a "Federal Character Principle" written into its constitution. This principle ensures that appointments to public service and government positions reflect the diversity of all states and ethnic groups, preventing domination by any single region.

Practice Questions

  1. What is the main difference between the Exclusive List and the Concurrent List in Nigeria's federal system?
  2. Explain why an independent judiciary is important in a federal system of government.
  3. Give one reason why some Nigerian states want to establish their own police force, and one reason the Federal Government opposes it.

Answers

  1. Answer: The Exclusive List contains matters that only the Federal Government can legislate on (e.g., defence, currency). The Concurrent List contains matters that both Federal and State governments can legislate on (e.g., education, health), but Federal law prevails if there is a conflict.
  2. Answer: An independent judiciary is needed to interpret the constitution and settle disputes between the federal and state governments impartially. Without it, one level of government could dominate the other, violating the federal balance.
  3. Answer: States want their own police to handle local security issues more effectively (e.g., farmer-herder clashes). The Federal Government opposes it because it fears ethnic or political misuse of state police and wants to maintain national security coordination.
