Pressure Groups in Government (SSS2)

Pressure groups are organized collections of individuals who seek to influence government policies and decisions without directly seeking political power. They are essential to democratic participation.

Key Characteristics & Methods

  • Organized membership: They have a defined membership base with common interests (e.g., labour unions, trade associations).
  • Non-partisan: They do not contest elections or seek to form a government; they only influence those in power.
  • Methods of influence: Lobbying, public campaigns, strikes, petitions, media advocacy, and litigation.
  • Types: Sectional (protect self-interest) vs. Promotional (advance a cause, e.g., environmental groups).
  • Importance: They ensure representation of diverse interests, check government power, and improve policy-making.

Nigerian Examples

1. Nigerian Labour Congress (NLC): A powerful trade union that organizes workers to demand better wages, working conditions, and social policies. It has organized nationwide strikes to pressure the government on minimum wage increases.

2. Arewa Consultative Forum (ACF): A socio-cultural pressure group that articulates the interests of the Northern region of Nigeria. It engages in dialogue with the government on issues like resource allocation, security, and political appointments.

🎉 Fun Fact

The term "lobbying" originated from the practice of interest groups waiting in the lobbies of the British Parliament to speak with Members of Parliament before votes. In Nigeria, the NLC’s 2012 "Occupy Nigeria" protests were so massive they forced the government to partially reverse a fuel subsidy removal!

Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: What is the primary difference between a pressure group and a political party?
    Answer: A pressure group seeks to influence government policies without contesting elections, while a political party aims to win elections and form a government.
  2. Question 2: Name two methods used by the Nigerian Labour Congress (NLC) to pressure the government.
    Answer: Organizing nationwide strikes and engaging in public protests (e.g., the 2012 "Occupy Nigeria" protests).
  3. Question 3: Give one reason why pressure groups are important in a democracy like Nigeria.
    Answer: They ensure that the interests of specific groups (e.g., workers, farmers, or regions) are heard by the government, promoting inclusive policy-making.
