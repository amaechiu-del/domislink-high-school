Key Concepts

  • Diode: A semiconductor device that allows current to flow in only one direction (forward bias). In reverse bias, it blocks current.
  • Rectification: The process of converting alternating current (AC) to direct current (DC) using diodes.
  • Half-wave rectification: Uses one diode to allow only the positive half of the AC cycle to pass, producing a pulsating DC output.
  • Forward bias: Anode positive relative to cathode → diode conducts.
  • Reverse bias: Anode negative relative to cathode → diode blocks current.

Nigerian Examples

Example 1: A technician in Lagos repairs a radio that uses a diode (e.g., 1N4007) to convert the AC from a step-down transformer into DC for the amplifier circuit. Without the diode, the radio produces only a loud hum.

Example 2: In a rural health centre in Kano, a solar-powered vaccine refrigerator uses a diode-based charge controller to prevent the battery from discharging back into the solar panel at night, protecting the vaccines.

🧠 Fun Fact

Light-emitting diodes (LEDs) are used in traffic lights across Abuja and Lagos. They last up to 50,000 hours — that's over 5 years of continuous operation! Unlike filament bulbs, LEDs don't burn out suddenly; they just get dimmer over time.

Practice Questions

  1. What is the main purpose of a diode in an electronic circuit?
  2. Explain why a half-wave rectifier produces a pulsating DC output instead of a steady DC voltage.
  3. If a diode is connected in reverse bias in a simple DC circuit (with a resistor and battery), what would you observe if you measure the current? Why?

Answers

  1. To allow current to flow in only one direction (rectification) and block current in the opposite direction.
  2. Because only the positive half-cycles of the AC input are allowed to pass through the diode; the negative half-cycles are blocked, resulting in a series of positive pulses (pulsating DC) rather than a constant voltage.
  3. No current would flow (or negligible leakage current). This is because in reverse bias, the diode's depletion region widens and prevents charge carriers from crossing the junction.
