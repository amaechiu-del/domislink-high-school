Optics: Reflection of Light

  • Law of Reflection: The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection. Both angles are measured from the normal (an imaginary line perpendicular to the reflecting surface).
  • Incident ray is the incoming light ray; reflected ray is the ray that bounces off the surface.
  • Reflection occurs when light strikes a smooth surface (like a plane mirror) and bounces back into the same medium.
  • Images formed by plane mirrors are virtual, upright, and laterally inverted (left-right reversed).
  • The image distance behind the mirror equals the object distance in front of it.

Nigerian Examples

Example 1: In Lagos, a tailor uses a large plane mirror in his shop to allow customers to see how a new agbada fits. The customer stands 1.5 m in front of the mirror. The image appears 1.5 m behind the mirror, giving a clear, upright view.

Example 2: A secondary school in Enugu uses a periscope made from two plane mirrors to demonstrate how light reflects at 45° angles. Students use it to see over a high fence during a practical physics class.

Fun Fact: The world's largest mirror is in the Sahara Desert! The Nigerian Optical Research Institute once calculated that a mirror the size of a football pitch in Abuja could reflect enough sunlight to power 2,000 street lamps.

Practice Questions

  1. A student stands 2 m in front of a plane mirror in a classroom in Kano. How far behind the mirror does his image appear?
  2. If a light ray hits a mirror at an angle of 40° to the mirror surface, what is the angle of reflection (measured from the normal)?
  3. Why is the image in a plane mirror called "virtual"?

Answers

  1. 2 m behind the mirror (image distance equals object distance).
  2. 50° (since angle to mirror = 40°, angle to normal = 90° - 40° = 50°; angle of incidence = 50°, so angle of reflection = 50°).
  3. Because the image cannot be projected on a screen — it appears to be behind the mirror but no light actually passes through that point.
