Ecosystems: Structure & Function

Biology (SSS2) – WAEC Syllabus

Key Concepts

  • Ecosystem: A community of living organisms interacting with their non-living environment (soil, water, air).
  • Producers: Green plants that convert sunlight into food via photosynthesis (e.g., grasses, trees).
  • Consumers: Organisms that feed on others. Primary consumers (herbivores) eat producers; secondary consumers (carnivores) eat primary consumers.
  • Decomposers: Bacteria and fungi that break down dead matter, returning nutrients to the soil.
  • Energy Flow: Energy moves from the sun → producers → consumers → decomposers. It is not recycled; it diminishes at each trophic level.
  • Nutrient Cycling: Matter (e.g., carbon, nitrogen) is recycled within the ecosystem.

Nigerian Examples

  • 🌴 Rainforest Ecosystem (Cross River State): Tall trees (e.g., Mahogany, Iroko) as producers, monkeys and birds as primary consumers, leopards as secondary consumers, and fungi as decomposers.
  • 🏝️ Mangrove Swamp Ecosystem (Niger Delta): Mangrove trees (producers), crabs and fish (primary consumers), crocodiles (secondary consumers), and bacteria (decomposers) thrive in salty, waterlogged soil.
💡 Fun Fact: The term "ecosystem" was first coined by British ecologist Arthur Tansley in 1935. In Nigeria, the Hadejia-Nguru Wetlands support over 1 million waterbirds annually, showing how fragile ecosystems can be vital for biodiversity!

Practice Questions

  1. Q1: Distinguish between a producer and a consumer in an ecosystem. Give one example of each from a Nigerian savanna.
  2. Q2: Explain why energy flow in an ecosystem is described as "one-way" while nutrients are recycled.
  3. Q3: A pond ecosystem contains algae (producer), tadpoles (primary consumer), and a heron (secondary consumer). If the algae store 1000 kJ of energy, how much energy is available to the heron? (Assume 10% transfer efficiency per trophic level).

Answers

  • A1: Producers (e.g., grass) make their own food via photosynthesis; consumers (e.g., zebra) cannot and must eat other organisms. In a Nigerian savanna: Andropogon grass (producer) and antelope (consumer).
  • A2: Energy flows one-way because it is lost as heat at each trophic level (e.g., respiration) and cannot be reused. Nutrients (e.g., carbon, nitrogen) are cycled through decomposition and uptake by plants.
  • A3: Algae (1000 kJ) → tadpoles (10% = 100 kJ) → heron (10% of 100 kJ = 10 kJ). So the heron gets 10 kJ.
