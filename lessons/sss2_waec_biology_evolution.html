Evolution: The Process of Change Over Time

Evolution is the gradual change in the inherited characteristics of biological populations over successive generations. It explains the diversity of life and the adaptation of organisms to their environment.

Key Concepts

  • Natural Selection: Organisms with traits better suited to their environment survive and reproduce more successfully.
  • Adaptation: Physical or behavioral features that help an organism survive (e.g., long neck of giraffe for reaching high leaves).
  • Variation: Differences in traits among individuals of the same species (e.g., different skin colours, beak shapes).
  • Speciation: Formation of new species when populations become isolated and evolve separately.

Nigerian Examples

  • Example 1 – Cassava Mealybug Resistance: In Nigeria, the cassava mealybug (Phenacoccus manihoti) devastated crops. Over time, some cassava varieties developed natural resistance through genetic variation and selection, helping farmers in the south-west reduce pesticide use.
  • Example 2 – Anopheles Mosquito Adaptation: In the Niger Delta region, the Anopheles gambiae mosquito has evolved resistance to DDT and other insecticides, a classic case of natural selection driven by human intervention.
🧬 Fun Fact: The Nigerian giraffe (West African giraffe) has a longer neck than its relatives in East Africa — an adaptation to reach leaves in the drier savanna regions of northern Nigeria!

Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: Explain how natural selection leads to the evolution of antibiotic resistance in bacteria.
    Answer: Bacteria with random mutations that make them resistant to antibiotics survive when antibiotics are used. They reproduce, passing on the resistance gene, and over time the population becomes mostly resistant.
  2. Question 2: In a population of grasshoppers in the savanna of northern Nigeria, green and brown varieties exist. Birds eat more brown grasshoppers. Which colour will become more common over generations? Why?
    Answer: Green will become more common because green grasshoppers are better camouflaged in the grass, so fewer are eaten. They survive longer and reproduce more, passing on the green colour allele.
  3. Question 3: Define the term "speciation" and give one example from Nigerian biodiversity.
    Answer: Speciation is the formation of new species due to isolation and divergent evolution. Example: The different species of cichlid fish in Lake Chad have evolved from a common ancestor due to isolation in different parts of the lake.
