Reproductive Systems: Male and Female Anatomy

SSS2 Biology – WAEC Syllabus

Key Components & Functions

  • Male: Testes produce sperm and testosterone; scrotum regulates temperature; vas deferens transports sperm; seminal vesicles and prostate add fluid to form semen; urethra carries semen/urine; penis deposits sperm.
  • Female: Ovaries produce eggs (ova) and estrogen; fallopian tubes (oviducts) transport eggs and are the site of fertilisation; uterus nourishes and houses the developing foetus; cervix opens into the vagina; vagina receives sperm and serves as birth canal.
  • Fertilisation: Occurs when sperm meets egg in the fallopian tube; the zygote then implants in the uterine wall.
  • Hormonal control: FSH and LH from the pituitary regulate the menstrual cycle and sperm production; testosterone and oestrogen control secondary sexual characteristics.

🇳🇬 Nigerian Examples

  1. Traditional fertility awareness: In many Nigerian communities, women monitor their menstrual cycles (e.g., using beads or calendar tracking) to identify the fertile window, especially in rural areas where access to modern contraception is limited.
  2. Male circumcision: Common among the Yoruba, Hausa, and Igbo as a cultural or religious practice; it involves removal of the foreskin of the penis and is sometimes performed in traditional ceremonies.

🎉 Fun Fact

The female ovary releases only one egg per month (about 400 in a lifetime), while the male testis produces over 1,000 sperm every second — that's more than 86 million per day!

📝 Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: Name the part of the female reproductive system where fertilisation typically occurs, and state one adaptation that aids this function.
  2. Question 2: Explain why the scrotum is located outside the body cavity in males, especially in a hot climate like Nigeria.
  3. Question 3: List two hormones involved in the menstrual cycle and describe the role of each.

Answers

  1. Answer 1: The fallopian tube (oviduct). It has a funnel-shaped fimbriae that sweeps the released egg into the tube, and its inner lining is ciliated to help move the egg toward the uterus.
  2. Answer 2: The scrotum hangs outside the body to keep the testes at a temperature about 2–3°C cooler than the body core. This is essential for sperm production, which is heat-sensitive. In Nigeria’s hot climate, the scrotum’s muscles (cremaster) relax or contract to adjust the distance from the body and regulate temperature.
  3. Answer 3: FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone) stimulates the growth of ovarian follicles and oestrogen production. LH (luteinising hormone) triggers ovulation and promotes the formation of the corpus luteum, which secretes progesterone to maintain the uterine lining.
