Key Concepts of Chemical Equilibrium

  • Reversible Reaction: A reaction that can proceed in both forward and backward directions simultaneously.
  • Dynamic Equilibrium: When the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction. Concentrations remain constant, not necessarily equal.
  • Le Chatelier's Principle: If a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it shifts to minimise the disturbance (change in concentration, temperature, or pressure).
  • Equilibrium Constant (Kc): For aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD, Kc = [C]c[D]d / [A]a[B]b (at constant temperature).

Nigerian Examples

  1. Haber Process in Kaduna Fertiliser Plant: N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g). High pressure (200 atm) and moderate temperature (450°C) are used to shift equilibrium toward ammonia production, vital for fertilisers used in Nigerian agriculture.
  2. Contact Process for Sulphuric Acid (used in Nigerian industries): 2SO₂(g) + O₂(g) ⇌ 2SO₃(g). Vanadium(V) oxide catalyst is used to speed up attainment of equilibrium without affecting the position.
💡 Fun Fact: Your blood relies on equilibrium! Haemoglobin (Hb) binds oxygen reversibly: Hb + 4O₂ ⇌ Hb(O₂)₄. At high altitudes (like Jos plateau), lower oxygen pressure shifts equilibrium to release more O₂ to tissues — that's why you breathe faster!

Practice Questions

  1. For the reaction: 2SO₂(g) + O₂(g) ⇌ 2SO₃(g) ΔH = -197 kJ/mol. What will be the effect of increasing temperature on the equilibrium position?
    Answer: The equilibrium shifts to the left (toward reactants) because the forward reaction is exothermic. Le Chatelier's principle: system opposes the added heat.
  2. Write the equilibrium constant expression (Kc) for: N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g).
    Answer: Kc = [NH₃]² / ([N₂][H₂]³)
  3. A mixture of N₂O₄ (colourless) and NO₂ (brown) is at equilibrium in a syringe. If the volume is suddenly decreased (pressure increased), what colour change is observed? (Reaction: N₂O₄(g) ⇌ 2NO₂(g))
    Answer: The colour becomes lighter (less brown) because increased pressure shifts equilibrium to the side with fewer gas molecules (left, toward colourless N₂O₄).
