Unemployment in Economics (SSS2 WAEC Syllabus)

Key Concepts

  • Unemployment – the state of being able and willing to work but unable to find a job at the prevailing wage rate.
  • Frictional unemployment – temporary unemployment while workers move between jobs or enter the labour force.
  • Structural unemployment – caused by a mismatch between workers’ skills and job requirements (e.g., obsolete industries).
  • Cyclical unemployment – linked to the business cycle; rises during recessions and falls during booms.
  • Seasonal unemployment – occurs when demand for labour varies with seasons (e.g., farming, tourism).
  • Underemployment – workers in jobs that do not fully use their skills or provide insufficient hours.

Nigerian Examples

Example 1: After the collapse of textile mills in Kano and Kaduna, many skilled weavers and factory workers could not find jobs because their skills were not needed in the growing tech/service sector. This is structural unemployment.

Example 2: During the 2020 COVID-19 recession, many Nigerian banks and airlines laid off staff due to reduced economic activity. As the economy recovered, some were rehired. This illustrates cyclical unemployment.

🎉 Fun Fact

Nigeria's National Bureau of Statistics (NBS) reported a youth unemployment rate (ages 15–34) of over 42% in 2023. That means nearly half of Nigeria’s young people who were actively seeking work could not find any! This is one of the highest rates in Africa.

Practice Questions

  1. Define unemployment and explain two types relevant to a developing country like Nigeria.
  2. Why might a graduate with a degree in history remain unemployed for many months? Which type of unemployment does this reflect?
  3. Using a real or hypothetical example, distinguish between cyclical and seasonal unemployment in the Nigerian context.

Answers

Answer 1: Unemployment is a situation where people who are willing and able to work cannot find jobs. Two types: Structural (e.g., oil boom makes farming less attractive, leaving rural workers idle) and Seasonal (e.g., farmers are idle after harvest until the next planting season).

Answer 2: A history graduate may face frictional unemployment if they are searching for a specific job, but more likely structural unemployment if their skills do not match available jobs (e.g., demand is for IT skills, not history).

Answer 3: Cyclical – during the 2016 recession, construction workers lost jobs as building projects halted; many were rehired when the economy grew. Seasonal – thousands of tomato farmers in Kano are jobless during the dry season (November–March) when irrigation is limited.
