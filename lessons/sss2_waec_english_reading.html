Reading Comprehension & Analysis (WAEC SSS2 English)

Key Skills for WAEC Reading Comprehension

  • Previewing: Quickly scan the passage to get a general idea of the topic, structure, and key points before reading in detail.
  • Scanning for Specifics: Look for dates, names, numbers, or specific words to answer factual questions without reading everything.
  • Identifying the Main Idea: Determine the central message or argument the author wants to convey. Often found in the first or last paragraph.
  • Understanding Vocabulary in Context: Use surrounding words and sentences to guess the meaning of unfamiliar terms.
  • Recognizing Tone and Mood: Note the author's attitude (e.g., critical, humorous, persuasive) through word choice and sentence structure.
  • Making Inferences: Read between the lines to understand ideas that are implied but not directly stated.
  • Summarizing: Condense the passage into a few sentences, capturing only the most important points.

Nigerian Context Examples

Example 1: Read this passage about “The Harmattan Season in Northern Nigeria”. A WAEC question might ask: “What is the main effect of the harmattan on farming activities?” To answer, scan for words like “crops,” “harvest,” or “dry season.” The main idea is often that the dry, dusty winds delay planting and reduce soil moisture.

Example 2: A passage about “The Role of Aso Ebi in Yoruba Weddings” may require you to infer the social meaning. A question could be: “Why do family members wear matching fabric?” The answer is not directly stated but inferred: it symbolizes unity, identity, and communal celebration.

🎉 Fun Fact: Did you know that the world’s longest novel is “Les Misérables” by Victor Hugo? It has over 1,500 pages! But for WAEC, you only need to read passages of about 500–700 words. Master the skills above, and you can tackle even the longest texts with confidence.

Practice Questions (WAEC Style)

Question 1: In a passage about the Nigerian civil service, the author writes: “The bureaucracy, though essential, is often hampered by red tape.” What does the phrase “red tape” most likely mean?

  • A) Official documents printed on red paper
  • B) Excessive rules and procedures
  • C) A type of adhesive tape
  • D) Government uniforms
Show Answer

Answer: B) Excessive rules and procedures. “Red tape” is an idiom meaning bureaucratic delay or rigid formalities.

Question 2: Read this sentence from a passage on Nigerian agriculture: “The adoption of drought-resistant cassava varieties has been a game-changer for farmers in the Sahel region.” What is the author’s tone?

  • A) Critical
  • B) Optimistic
  • C) Sarcastic
  • D) Indifferent
Show Answer

Answer: B) Optimistic. The phrase “game-changer” suggests a positive and hopeful outlook.

Question 3: A passage describes a Lagos traffic scene: “Danfo drivers weave through the gridlock with practiced ease, their horns a symphony of impatience.” Which literary device is used in “symphony of impatience”?

  • A) Simile
  • B) Metaphor
  • C) Oxymoron
  • D) Alliteration
Show Answer

Answer: B) Metaphor. The horn sounds are compared to a symphony without using “like” or “as,” creating a vivid contrast between chaos and harmony.
