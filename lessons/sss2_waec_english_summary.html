Mastering Summary Writing: SSS2 English (WAEC Syllabus)

Key Steps for Summary Writing

  • Read the passage twice – first for general meaning, second to locate main ideas.
  • Identify the topic sentence in each paragraph; it often contains the core idea.
  • Underline or note key points – ignore examples, repetitions, and minor details.
  • Paraphrase – rewrite the ideas in your own words without changing the meaning.
  • Condense – combine related points and remove unnecessary words (e.g., “the fact that” → “that”).
  • Count your words – WAEC often requires a specific word limit (e.g., 100 words).
  • Use linking words sparingly (e.g., “also,” “however”) to maintain flow.
  • Write in one paragraph unless the question specifies otherwise.

Nigerian Examples

Example 1: Agriculture (WAEC-style)

Original Passage: "Nigeria's agricultural sector employs over 35% of the workforce, yet contributes less than 25% to the GDP. The government has introduced various policies to boost food production, including the Anchor Borrowers' Programme, which provides loans to small-scale farmers. However, challenges like poor storage facilities and climate change still hinder progress."

Summary: Agriculture employs 35% of Nigeria's workforce but contributes under 25% to GDP. Government initiatives like the Anchor Borrowers' Programme aim to increase production, though poor storage and climate change remain obstacles.

Example 2: Education

Original Passage: "The Universal Basic Education (UBE) programme in Nigeria was launched to provide free, compulsory education for children aged 6 to 15. Despite this, many children in rural areas still drop out due to poverty and lack of schools. Non-governmental organizations have stepped in to build classrooms and provide scholarships, but funding remains inadequate."

Summary: Nigeria's UBE programme offers free education for children 6–15, but rural dropouts persist due to poverty and insufficient schools. NGOs help with infrastructure and scholarships, yet funding gaps remain.

Practice Questions

Question 1

Passage: "The harmattan season in Nigeria brings dry, dusty winds from the Sahara. This causes low humidity, cracked skin, and respiratory issues for many people. Farmers often lose crops due to the lack of moisture, while schools in northern states sometimes close because of poor visibility."

Task: Summarize the passage in one sentence.

Click for Answer

The harmattan season in Nigeria causes dry winds, health problems, crop loss, and school closures due to dust and low humidity.

Question 2

Passage: "Lagos is Nigeria's largest city and economic hub, but it faces severe traffic congestion. The government has introduced a Bus Rapid Transit (BRT) system to ease movement. However, many residents still prefer private cars due to inadequate BRT coverage and irregular schedules."

Task: Write a summary of the passage in two sentences.

Click for Answer

Lagos, Nigeria's economic hub, suffers from heavy traffic despite the BRT system. Many residents avoid BRT because of limited routes and unreliable timing.

Question 3

Passage: "Yoruba traditional rulers, known as Obas, play a crucial role in preserving culture and settling disputes. They are respected as custodians of tradition, but their political power has diminished since colonial rule. Today, they serve mainly as ceremonial figures and advisors."

Task: Summarize the passage in 30 words or fewer.

Click for Answer

Yoruba Obas preserve culture and settle disputes, but their political power declined after colonial rule; they now serve ceremonial roles.
