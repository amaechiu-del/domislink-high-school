Probability (SSS2 Mathematics - WAEC Syllabus)

Key Concepts

  • Probability measures the likelihood of an event happening, expressed as a number between 0 and 1 (or 0% to 100%).
  • Sample Space (S): The set of all possible outcomes in an experiment.
  • Event (E): A subset of the sample space — the outcome(s) we are interested in.
  • Formula: P(E) = (Number of favourable outcomes) / (Total number of possible outcomes).
  • If P(E) = 0, the event is impossible; if P(E) = 1, the event is certain.
  • For mutually exclusive events, P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B).

Nigerian Examples

Example 1: Lagos Traffic Lights
At a major junction in Lagos, a traffic light shows red for 30 seconds, yellow for 5 seconds, and green for 25 seconds in each cycle. What is the probability that a driver arriving at random will meet a green light?

Solution: Total cycle time = 30 + 5 + 25 = 60 seconds. Favourable time for green = 25 seconds. P(green) = 25/60 = 5/12.

Example 2: National Youth Service Corps (NYSC) Posting
A batch of 200 corps members includes 80 from the South-West, 50 from the North-Central, 40 from the South-East, and 30 from the North-East. If one corps member is selected at random, what is the probability that the person is from the South-East or North-East?

Solution: Total = 200. South-East = 40, North-East = 30. Favourable = 40 + 30 = 70. P = 70/200 = 7/20.

🎲 Fun Fact: The word "probability" comes from the Latin probabilitas, which originally meant "worthiness" or "credibility". In 1654, French mathematicians Blaise Pascal and Pierre de Fermat laid the foundation of modern probability theory while solving a gambling problem about dice!

Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: A bag contains 4 red balls, 3 blue balls, and 5 green balls. If one ball is picked at random, what is the probability that it is not blue?
    Answer: Total balls = 12. Not blue = 4+5 = 9. P(not blue) = 9/12 = 3/4.
  2. Question 2: In a class of 40 SSS2 students, 18 are boys and 22 are girls. If a student is chosen at random, what is the probability that the student is a girl?
    Answer: P(girl) = 22/40 = 11/20.
  3. Question 3: A fair six-sided die is rolled once. What is the probability of rolling a number greater than 4?
    Answer: Numbers greater than 4 are 5 and 6 (2 outcomes). Total outcomes = 6. P = 2/6 = 1/3.
