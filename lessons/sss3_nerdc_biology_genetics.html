Key Concepts in Genetics

  • Gene – A unit of heredity on a chromosome that controls a specific trait (e.g., seed colour).
  • Allele – Different versions of the same gene (e.g., G for green pods, g for yellow pods).
  • Genotype – The genetic makeup of an organism (e.g., GG, Gg, gg).
  • Phenotype – The observable physical trait (e.g., green or yellow pods).
  • Dominant allele – An allele that masks the expression of another (written in capital letter).
  • Recessive allele – An allele expressed only when two copies are present (written in small letter).
  • Homozygous – Having two identical alleles for a trait (GG or gg).
  • Heterozygous – Having two different alleles for a trait (Gg).

Nigerian Examples

  1. Cassava (Manihot esculenta) – In Nigeria, cassava varieties bred for high starch content (dominant trait) are crossed with local low-starch varieties (recessive). The F1 generation shows all high-starch plants, confirming dominance.
  2. Cowpea (Vigna unguiculata) – Nigerian farmers select cowpeas for pest resistance. The resistance allele (R) is dominant over susceptibility (r). A cross between RR and rr yields all Rr resistant plants in the first generation.
🧬 Fun Fact: The term "genetics" was coined by the British biologist William Bateson in 1905. He also introduced the words "allele", "homozygous", and "heterozygous"! In Nigeria, genetic research at IITA (Ibadan) has developed over 50 improved cassava varieties since 1970.

Practice Questions

  1. In guinea pigs, black fur (B) is dominant over white fur (b). If a heterozygous black guinea pig is crossed with a white guinea pig, what percentage of the offspring will be black?
  2. In garden peas, tall stem (T) is dominant over short stem (t). What are the possible genotypes of a tall pea plant? Explain.
  3. A farmer in Kano crosses a homozygous red-flowered sorghum (RR) with a homozygous white-flowered sorghum (rr). All F1 plants have red flowers. If the F1 plants are self-pollinated, what ratio of flower colours would you expect in the F2 generation?

Answers

  1. 50% – The cross is Bb × bb. Offspring: 2 Bb (black) and 2 bb (white) = 50% black.
  2. TT or Tt – A tall plant can be homozygous dominant (TT) or heterozygous (Tt). Both genotypes produce the tall phenotype because T is dominant.
  3. 3 red : 1 white – F1 are all Rr. Self-pollination gives RR (red), Rr (red), Rr (red), rr (white) = 3:1 ratio.
