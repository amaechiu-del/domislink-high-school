Sense Organs: The Body's Window to the World

Topic: Biology (NERDC Syllabus) | Class: SSS3

Key Sense Organs & Their Functions

  • Eye (Vision): Detects light and color. Contains the retina (photoreceptors: rods for dim light, cones for color). The lens focuses light onto the retina.
  • Ear (Hearing & Balance): Outer ear collects sound waves; middle ear amplifies them via ossicles; inner ear (cochlea) converts vibrations to nerve signals. Semicircular canals maintain equilibrium.
  • Nose (Smell): Olfactory epithelium in the nasal cavity detects airborne chemicals. Signals travel via olfactory nerve to the brain.
  • Tongue (Taste): Taste buds on papillae detect five basic tastes: sweet, sour, salty, bitter, and umami.
  • Skin (Touch): Largest sense organ. Contains mechanoreceptors for pressure, thermoreceptors for temperature, and nociceptors for pain.

Nigerian Examples

  1. Egusi Soup & Taste: When tasting egusi soup, the tongue's taste buds detect the rich umami from melon seeds, the saltiness from seasoning, and the slight bitterness from bitter leaf — all at once.
  2. Harmattan & Skin: During the dry, dusty Harmattan season in Northern Nigeria, the skin's thermoreceptors sense the cold, dry air, while touch receptors feel the fine dust particles settling on the skin.
🧠 Fun Fact: The human eye can distinguish about 10 million different colors! However, the nose can detect over 1 trillion different scents — making smell far more sensitive than many people think.

Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: Which part of the ear is responsible for maintaining body balance?
    Answer: The semicircular canals (in the inner ear).
  2. Question 2: Name the two types of photoreceptor cells in the retina and state one function of each.
    Answer: Rods — sensitive to dim light (night vision); Cones — responsible for color vision in bright light.
  3. Question 3: During a traditional Nigerian pepper soup tasting, which sense organ is primarily used to detect the spicy heat?
    Answer: The skin (touch) — specifically, pain receptors (nociceptors) in the mouth and tongue respond to capsaicin, the compound that makes pepper "hot."
