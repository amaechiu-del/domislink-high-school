Creative Writing: Crafting Compelling Narratives

Key Elements of Creative Writing

  • Imagery – Use vivid sensory details (sight, sound, smell, taste, touch) to paint pictures in the reader's mind.
  • Dialogue – Realistic conversation between characters reveals personality and advances the story.
  • Plot – A structured sequence of events: exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, resolution.
  • Voice – The unique style and perspective of the writer or narrator—what makes the story feel authentic.
  • Setting – Time and place that shape the mood and context of the narrative.
  • Theme – The underlying message or central idea (e.g., love, betrayal, resilience).

Nigerian Examples

Example 1 (Imagery & Setting):
“The harmattan wind carried the scent of roasting plantains from Mama Nkechi’s roadside stall. Dust swirled in golden spirals around the legs of children playing football with a bundle of rags.”
— This passage uses sensory details (smell, sight) to bring a typical Nigerian harmattan afternoon to life.

Example 2 (Dialogue & Voice):
“Oga, wetin you dey find for this market?” the trader called out, her voice rising above the blare of a danfo horn. “I get fine Ankara wey go make your wife shine like Christmas!”
— The dialogue captures the energy and pidgin-rich speech of a Lagos market scene.

🎉 Fun Fact: Nigerian author Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie wrote her first novel, Purple Hibiscus, at just 25 years old. She said she drew inspiration from the rhythms of Igbo storytelling and the everyday voices of people in Nsukka.

Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: Write a short paragraph (3–5 sentences) describing a busy motor park in Lagos using at least three sensory details.
  2. Question 2: Create a brief dialogue between a farmer and a trader in a village market. Let the exchange reveal something about the farmer’s harvest.
  3. Question 3: Identify the theme in this sentence: “Despite the flood that swept away their cassava farm, the family gathered under the mango tree and shared the last yam.”

Answers

Answer 1 (Sample): The air was thick with diesel fumes and the shouts of conductors calling “Ojuelegba! Ojuelegba!” A woman balanced a tray of oranges on her head, while a bus driver revved his engine, sending a cloud of black smoke into the dusty afternoon.

Answer 2 (Sample):
Farmer: “Oya, see this yam—big pass your head! I dig am yesterday.”
Trader: “Ehn? Your yam sweet? Last time your yam dey bitter o.”
Farmer: “Ah, my sister, try am. If e bitter, I go give you double your money back.”

Answer 3: The theme is resilience or unity in hardship—the family shares what little they have despite loss.
