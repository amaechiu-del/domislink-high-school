Key Points for Clear Oral English (SSS3 NERDC)

  • Vowel Clarity: Nigerian English often shortens vowels (e.g., "seat" vs "sit"). Practice holding long vowels like /iː/ (beet) and /uː/ (boot).
  • Consonant Pairs: Distinguish between /θ/ (think) and /t/ (tink), and /ð/ (this) and /d/ (dis). Place tongue between teeth for /θ/ and /ð/.
  • Word Stress: Stress the correct syllable. e.g., CONtract (noun) vs conTRACT (verb). Wrong stress changes meaning.
  • Intonation: Rising tone for yes/no questions ("You are coming?" ↗), falling tone for WH-questions ("Where are you going?" ↘).
  • Connected Speech: Words link naturally — "What are you doing?" becomes "Wha-cha doin'?" in rapid speech.

🇳🇬 Nigerian Examples

Example 1: "I am going to the market." (Many Nigerians say "mar-KET" with stress on the second syllable. Correct: "MAR-ket" — stress on first syllable.)

Example 2: "She tink about it." (Should be "think" — the /θ/ sound requires the tongue tip between the teeth, not /t/.)

💡 Fun Fact

English has about 44 distinct sounds (phonemes), but only 26 letters. That's why one letter can represent many sounds — like the letter "a" in father (/ɑː/), cat (/æ/), and cake (/eɪ/). In many Nigerian languages, vowels are pure and unchanging, so English diphthongs (gliding vowels) require extra practice!

Practice Questions

  1. Which word has a different vowel sound? beat, bit, bet, bat
  2. Correct the stress in this sentence: "I need to re-CORD the mu-SIC."
  3. Identify the consonant sound common to all: thin, this, father, weather

✅ Answers

  1. bat — /æ/ sound; the others have /iː/, /ɪ/, /ɛ/ respectively.
  2. "I need to RE-cord the MU-sic." (Record = RE-cord as noun; music = MU-sic.)
  3. The /ð/ sound (voiced "th") — all four words use the voiced dental fricative.
