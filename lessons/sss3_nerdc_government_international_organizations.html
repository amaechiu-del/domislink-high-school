International Organizations (Government – SSS3)

Key Features of International Organizations

  • Membership: Usually composed of sovereign states that voluntarily join.
  • Objectives: Promote peace, security, economic cooperation, and human rights.
  • Structure: Have permanent secretariats, decision-making bodies (e.g., General Assembly, Security Council).
  • Legality: Operate under international treaties or charters.
  • Sovereignty: Member states retain sovereignty but agree to abide by collective decisions.

Nigerian Examples

🇳🇬 Example 1 – Nigeria in the African Union (AU): Nigeria played a key role in the formation of the AU (formerly OAU) and has contributed troops to AU peacekeeping missions in Darfur and Somalia.

🇳🇬 Example 2 – Nigeria in ECOWAS: As a founding member of the Economic Community of West African States, Nigeria has led interventions in Liberia and Sierra Leone (ECOMOG) and promotes regional trade integration.

💡 Fun Fact: The United Nations was founded in 1945 with 51 member states. Today it has 193 members – but Nigeria was among the first African countries to join, becoming a member in 1960 right after independence!

Practice Questions

  1. What is the primary difference between a global international organization (e.g., UN) and a regional one (e.g., ECOWAS)?
  2. Name two specific ways Nigeria has benefited from its membership in the African Union.
  3. Explain why international organizations like OPEC can influence the economy of member countries like Nigeria.

📝 Answers

  1. Global organizations have worldwide membership and address universal issues (peace, climate), while regional organizations focus on a specific geographic area and its unique challenges (e.g., economic integration in West Africa via ECOWAS).
  2. Nigeria has received support for infrastructure projects through the AU's New Partnership for Africa's Development (NEPAD) and has gained diplomatic backing on issues like the fight against Boko Haram through AU security frameworks.
  3. OPEC coordinates oil production quotas among member countries. As a major oil exporter, Nigeria's revenue and budget are directly affected by OPEC's decisions on output levels, which influence global oil prices.
