Nigerian Foreign Policy: Principles & Practice

Key Principles

  • Afrocentrism – Africa remains the centrepiece of Nigeria’s foreign policy. Nigeria champions decolonisation, African unity (OAU/AU), and regional stability.
  • Non-Alignment – During the Cold War, Nigeria refused to join either the Western or Eastern blocs, maintaining independence in international affairs.
  • Peaceful Coexistence – Nigeria advocates for conflict resolution through dialogue, diplomacy, and multilateralism (e.g., UN, ECOWAS).
  • Economic Diplomacy – Since the 1990s, Nigeria has focused on attracting foreign investment, trade partnerships, and diaspora engagement.
  • Respect for Sovereignty – Non-interference in internal affairs of other states, except in cases of gross human rights violations (e.g., apartheid).

🇳🇬 Nigerian Examples in Practice

Example 1: ECOMOG & Liberia (1990s)
Nigeria contributed over 70% of troops and funding to the ECOWAS Monitoring Group (ECOMOG) during the Liberian civil war. This demonstrated Afrocentrism and a commitment to regional peace, despite heavy financial cost.

Example 2: Technical Aid Corps (TAC) Scheme
Established in 1987, Nigeria sends professionals (doctors, teachers, engineers) to African, Caribbean, and Pacific countries. This fosters goodwill and showcases Nigeria’s leadership in South-South cooperation.

🎉 Fun Fact

Nigeria is the only African country that has served on the UN Security Council four times (1966–67, 1978–79, 1994–95, 2010–11). This reflects its consistent diplomatic activism and global recognition.

📝 Practice Questions

  1. What does “Afrocentrism” mean in the context of Nigerian foreign policy?
    Answer: It means that Africa is the central focus of Nigeria’s external relations—prioritising African unity, decolonisation, and regional peace.
  2. Why did Nigeria adopt a policy of non-alignment during the Cold War?
    Answer: To maintain independence from both the US and Soviet blocs, avoid proxy conflicts, and focus on national development and African liberation struggles.
  3. Give one example of Nigeria using economic diplomacy.
    Answer: The Technical Aid Corps (TAC) scheme, where Nigerian experts are sent abroad, strengthening bilateral ties and trade opportunities.
