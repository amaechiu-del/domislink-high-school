Electromagnetism: The Unified Force

Electromagnetism describes the interaction between electric currents and magnetic fields. A changing magnetic field induces an electric current, and an electric current generates a magnetic field.

  • Oersted’s Principle: A current-carrying conductor creates a magnetic field around it. The direction is given by the right-hand grip rule.
  • Electromagnet: A coil of wire (solenoid) wrapped around a soft iron core. When current flows, the core becomes magnetized; when current stops, it loses magnetism.
  • Factors affecting strength: Increase number of turns, increase current, or use a ferromagnetic core (e.g., soft iron) to concentrate the field.
  • Applications: Electric bells, relays, magnetic cranes, and MRI machines all rely on electromagnets.

🇳🇬 Nigerian Examples

1. Scrap Metal Separation in Lagos: A recycling company in Ikeja uses a powerful electromagnet to lift and separate ferrous metals (iron, steel) from non-ferrous waste. The magnet is switched on to pick up metals and switched off to drop them into separate bins.

2. Electric Bell in a Kaduna School: When the bell button is pressed, current flows through an electromagnet, attracting a metal striker to hit the bell. The circuit breaks automatically, the magnet releases, and the cycle repeats — producing the ringing sound.

⚡ Fun Fact: The world’s most powerful electromagnet is at the National High Magnetic Field Laboratory in Florida, USA. It produces a magnetic field of 45 Tesla — nearly a million times stronger than Earth’s magnetic field! That’s strong enough to levitate a frog!

📝 Practice Questions

  1. State three ways to increase the strength of an electromagnet.
  2. An electric bell uses an electromagnet. Explain briefly how the bell produces a continuous ringing sound.
  3. A student winds 50 turns of wire around a nail and connects it to a 1.5 V battery. The nail picks up 5 paper clips. What two changes could the student make to pick up 15 paper clips?

Answers:

  1. Increase the number of turns of the coil; increase the current (use a higher voltage battery); insert a soft iron core.
  2. The electromagnet attracts a metal striker to hit the bell. The contact breaks, the magnet releases, the striker returns, and the circuit remakes — this oscillates rapidly, producing ringing.
  3. Increase the number of turns (e.g., to 150 turns) or use a higher voltage battery (e.g., 3 V) to increase current.
