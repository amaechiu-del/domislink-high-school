Semiconductors (SSS3 Physics - NERDC Syllabus)

Key Concepts

  • Semiconductors are materials with electrical conductivity between conductors (e.g., copper) and insulators (e.g., rubber). Examples: Silicon (Si) and Germanium (Ge).
  • P-type semiconductors are doped with elements that create "holes" (positive charge carriers). Doping with Boron (Group III) produces holes.
  • N-type semiconductors are doped with elements that provide extra electrons. Doping with Phosphorus (Group V) adds free electrons.
  • P-N Junction is formed when P-type and N-type materials are joined. It allows current to flow easily in one direction (forward bias) but blocks it in the opposite direction (reverse bias).
  • Applications: Diodes, transistors, solar cells, and light-emitting diodes (LEDs).

🇳🇬 Nigerian Examples

  1. Solar-Powered Streetlights in Lagos: Many streetlights in Lagos use solar panels made from semiconductor materials (silicon). The P-N junctions in the solar cells convert sunlight into electricity, powering LED bulbs at night.
  2. Mobile Phone Chargers (Abuja Markets): The rectifier diodes inside phone chargers sold in Wuse Market contain semiconductor P-N junctions. They convert AC mains power to DC power for charging phones.

⚡ Fun Fact: The first transistor (made of germanium) was invented in 1947 at Bell Labs. Today, a single computer chip can contain billions of tiny semiconductor transistors — smaller than a grain of sand!

Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: What are the majority charge carriers in a P-type semiconductor?
    Answer: Holes (positive charge carriers).
  2. Question 2: A P-N junction is connected so that the P-side is positive and the N-side is negative. Is this forward bias or reverse bias?
    Answer: Forward bias (current flows easily).
  3. Question 3: Name one common semiconductor element used in solar cells in Nigeria.
    Answer: Silicon (Si).
