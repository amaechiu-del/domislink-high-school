Environmental Biology: Ecosystem Dynamics & Conservation

Key Concepts

  • Ecosystem: A community of living organisms (biotic) interacting with their non-living (abiotic) environment (e.g., soil, water, air).
  • Energy Flow: Energy from the sun is captured by producers (plants) during photosynthesis and transferred through food chains to consumers and decomposers.
  • Trophic Levels: Producers (autotrophs) → Primary consumers (herbivores) → Secondary consumers (carnivores) → Tertiary consumers → Decomposers.
  • Nutrient Cycling: Elements like carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus are recycled through biogeochemical cycles (e.g., carbon cycle, nitrogen cycle).
  • Ecological Succession: The gradual change in species composition of an ecosystem over time (primary vs. secondary succession).
  • Conservation: Sustainable management of natural resources to prevent extinction, habitat loss, and pollution.

Nigerian Examples

1. The Niger Delta Mangrove Ecosystem: This rich coastal habitat supports fish, crabs, and birds like the African fish eagle. It acts as a carbon sink and protects shorelines. Oil pollution and deforestation threaten this ecosystem, highlighting the need for conservation.

2. The Jos Plateau (Savanna Ecosystem): Characterized by grasses and scattered trees, it supports grazing animals like cattle and wildlife. Overgrazing and bush burning have led to desertification in some areas, demonstrating the impact of human activities on nutrient cycling and soil fertility.

🌟 Fun Fact!

Did you know? The Moringa tree (Zogale in Hausa) is a "miracle tree" in Nigeria. It can purify water, provide nutrients, and its leaves are used to combat malnutrition—showing how a single species can support both human health and environmental sustainability!

Practice Questions (WAEC-Style)

  1. Question 1: In a food chain, which trophic level is responsible for converting solar energy into chemical energy?
    Answer: Producers (autotrophs, e.g., green plants).
  2. Question 2: Name two human activities that disrupt the carbon cycle in Nigerian ecosystems.
    Answer: Deforestation (cutting down forests for agriculture) and burning of fossil fuels (e.g., in generators or vehicles).
  3. Question 3: What is the difference between primary and secondary ecological succession? Give one Nigerian example of each.
    Answer: Primary succession occurs on bare rock or areas with no soil (e.g., new volcanic rock on the Jos Plateau). Secondary succession occurs on sites where soil remains after a disturbance (e.g., farmland abandoned after overgrazing in northern Nigeria).
