Amines: Chemistry of Organic Nitrogen Compounds

Key Explanations

  • Amines are derivatives of ammonia (NH₃) where one or more hydrogen atoms are replaced by alkyl or aryl groups.
  • Classification: Based on the number of hydrogen atoms replaced — primary (1°), secondary (2°), or tertiary (3°).
  • Basicity: Amines are weak bases due to the lone pair of electrons on nitrogen. Aliphatic amines are more basic than aromatic amines.
  • Hydrogen bonding: Primary and secondary amines can form intermolecular hydrogen bonds, giving higher boiling points than alkanes of similar mass.
  • Solubility: Small amines (e.g., methylamine) are soluble in water due to hydrogen bonding; solubility decreases as the carbon chain lengthens.

Nigerian Examples

🇳🇬 1. Trimethylamine in Fish Odor: In coastal regions of Nigeria, spoiling fish (e.g., Croaker or Titus) releases trimethylamine, a tertiary amine responsible for the characteristic "fishy" smell. This is why fresh fish is often washed with acidic lime juice — to neutralize the amine.

🇳🇬 2. Aniline in Dye Production: Aniline (phenylamine) is a primary aromatic amine used in the Nigerian textile industry for making azo dyes. Local artisans in places like Kano use aniline derivatives to produce vibrant colours for traditional fabrics like aso oke.

🎉 Fun Fact: The smell of pawpaw (papaya) contains trace amounts of amines! Also, the venom of the Nigerian carpet viper contains amine-based compounds that affect nerve signals — nature's own amine chemistry!

Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: Classify the following amine: CH₃CH₂NHCH₃.
    Answer: Secondary (2°) amine — the nitrogen is bonded to two alkyl groups (ethyl and methyl) and one hydrogen.
  2. Question 2: Why is methylamine (CH₃NH₂) more basic than aniline (C₆H₅NH₂)?
    Answer: In aniline, the lone pair on nitrogen is delocalized into the benzene ring (resonance), making it less available for protonation. In methylamine, the lone pair is localized on nitrogen, so it is a stronger base.
  3. Question 3: Which amine has a higher boiling point: ethylamine (CH₃CH₂NH₂) or trimethylamine ((CH₃)₃N)? Explain.
    Answer: Ethylamine has a higher boiling point because it is a primary amine and can form strong intermolecular hydrogen bonds (N–H⋯N). Trimethylamine is tertiary — no N–H bonds — so only weak van der Waals forces exist, giving a lower boiling point.
