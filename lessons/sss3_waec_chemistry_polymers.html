Polymers: The Giants of Chemistry

Syllabus: WAEC Chemistry (SSS3) – Types of polymers, polymerization, and everyday applications.

  • Polymers are large molecules made up of many smaller repeating units called monomers.
  • Addition polymerization: monomers join without losing any atoms (e.g., ethene → polyethene).
  • Condensation polymerization: monomers join with the loss of small molecules like water (e.g., nylon formation).
  • Polymers can be natural (e.g., starch, cellulose, proteins) or synthetic (e.g., plastics, nylon, rubber).
  • Properties depend on chain length, cross-linking, and functional groups.

🇳🇬 Nigerian Examples

1. Natural rubber from Hevea brasiliensis – tapped from rubber trees in Edo, Delta, and Ondo states; used in tyres, footwear, and industrial products.

2. Polyethylene (nylon) bags – widely used in markets across Nigeria for packaging; made from synthetic polymers derived from petroleum.

🎉 Fun Fact

The DNA in your body is a natural polymer! Each nucleotide is a monomer, and the double helix is a giant polymer chain that carries your genetic code.

📝 Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: What is the name of the process by which monomers join to form a polymer with the loss of a small molecule like water?
  2. Question 2: Give two examples of natural polymers found in living organisms.
  3. Question 3: Which type of polymerization does ethene (C₂H₄) undergo to form polyethene?

✅ Answers

  1. Answer 1: Condensation polymerization (or step-growth polymerization).
  2. Answer 2: Starch, cellulose, proteins, DNA, rubber (any two).
  3. Answer 3: Addition polymerization (since no by-product is lost).
