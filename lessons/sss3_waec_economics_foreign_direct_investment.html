Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) – SSS3 Economics (WAEC Syllabus)

Definition: Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) is an investment made by a firm or individual from one country into business interests located in another country. Unlike foreign portfolio investment, FDI implies a long-term relationship and control or significant influence over the enterprise.

Key Features of FDI

  • Long-term commitment: FDI involves lasting interest and control (usually >10% equity).
  • Transfer of resources: Includes capital, technology, management skills, and market access.
  • Greenfield vs Brownfield: Greenfield = building new facilities; Brownfield = acquiring/merging existing firms.
  • Host country benefits: Job creation, infrastructure development, increased tax revenue, and technology spillovers.
  • Investor benefits: Access to raw materials, cheaper labour, new markets, and reduced trade barriers.

Types of FDI

  • Horizontal FDI: A firm duplicates its home-country operations in a foreign country (e.g., a carmaker building a factory abroad).
  • Vertical FDI: A firm moves part of its supply chain abroad (e.g., sourcing raw materials or assembling components).
  • Conglomerate FDI: Investment in a completely different business line in a foreign country.

Nigerian Examples

1. MTN Nigeria (Telecommunications)
MTN Group, a South African company, invested billions of dollars in Nigeria to build network infrastructure (Greenfield FDI). This created thousands of jobs and revolutionized mobile communication in Nigeria.

2. Dangote Refinery (Brownfield & Greenfield)
Though Dangote is Nigerian, the refinery attracted significant FDI from international partners and lenders (e.g., Afreximbank). It is the largest single-train refinery in the world, reducing Nigeria’s dependence on imported fuel.

💡 Fun Fact: In 2022, Nigeria was the largest recipient of FDI in West Africa, attracting over $1.8 billion. However, this was still lower than pre-pandemic levels due to global economic shifts.

Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: Distinguish between Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) and Foreign Portfolio Investment (FPI).
  2. Question 2: Explain two ways FDI can benefit the Nigerian economy.
  3. Question 3: A Nigerian company builds a new cement factory in Ghana. Is this an example of inward or outward FDI for Nigeria? Justify your answer.

Answers (for self-check)

Answer 1: FDI involves long-term control and active management of a foreign enterprise (usually >10% equity), while FPI is passive investment in stocks/bonds without control. FDI brings technology and jobs; FPI is more liquid and short-term.

Answer 2: (i) Job creation – e.g., MTN’s investment employed thousands of Nigerians. (ii) Technology transfer – e.g., new machinery and managerial techniques improve local productivity.

Answer 3: This is outward FDI for Nigeria, because a Nigerian firm is investing in a foreign country (Ghana). For Ghana, it is inward FDI.
