Globalization in Economics (SSS3 WAEC Syllabus)

Key Concepts of Globalization

  • Definition: Globalization is the increasing interdependence of economies worldwide through cross-border trade, investment, technology, and information flows.
  • Trade Liberalization: Reduction of tariffs and non-tariff barriers to encourage free trade between nations.
  • Foreign Direct Investment (FDI): Multinational corporations invest in other countries, creating jobs and transferring skills.
  • Technology Transfer: Spread of innovations, communication tools, and production methods across borders.
  • Cultural Exchange: Spread of ideas, values, and consumer habits (e.g., fast food, fashion).
  • Financial Integration: Global capital markets allow money to move quickly between countries for investment or speculation.

🇳🇬 Nigerian Examples

  1. MTN Nigeria: A South African multinational telecom company operating in Nigeria. It brought mobile technology, created over 1,000 direct jobs, and contributed to Nigeria's GDP while repatriating profits to South Africa.
  2. Nigerian Oil Exports: Nigeria exports crude oil to global markets (e.g., Europe, India, USA). This integration makes Nigeria vulnerable to global oil price fluctuations, but also provides major foreign exchange earnings.

🎉 Fun Fact: In 2023, Nigeria was the largest economy in Africa by GDP, yet its share of global trade was less than 0.5%. This shows how globalization offers both opportunities (access to global markets) and challenges (competition from larger economies).

📝 Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: Define globalization and list three ways it affects developing economies like Nigeria.
  2. Question 2: Explain how foreign direct investment (FDI) can be both beneficial and harmful to a country's economy, using a Nigerian example.
  3. Question 3: What is trade liberalization? Describe one advantage and one disadvantage for Nigerian farmers.

✅ Answers

  1. Answer 1: Globalization is the growing interdependence of world economies. Effects: (i) Increased foreign investment (e.g., MTN in Nigeria). (ii) Exposure to global competition, which can hurt local industries. (iii) Access to new technology and ideas.
  2. Answer 2: FDI brings capital, jobs, and technology (benefit), but profits are often repatriated, reducing local reinvestment (harm). Example: Shell's oil operations in the Niger Delta created jobs but also led to environmental degradation and limited local control.
  3. Answer 3: Trade liberalization means reducing trade barriers. Advantage: Nigerian farmers can export agricultural products (e.g., cocoa) more easily. Disadvantage: They face competition from subsidized foreign imports (e.g., rice from Thailand), which can lower local prices and hurt incomes.
