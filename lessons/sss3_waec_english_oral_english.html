Key Concepts in Oral English for WAEC

  • Intonation – the rise and fall of the voice when speaking. Rising intonation (↗) is used for yes/no questions; falling intonation (↘) for statements and commands.
  • Stress – the emphasis placed on a syllable or word. In Nigerian English, correct stress helps clarity (e.g., PHOto vs. phoTOgraph).
  • Rhythm – the pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables. English is a stress-timed language, meaning stressed syllables occur at roughly equal intervals.
  • Connected Speech – linking words together (e.g., "What are you" → "Whatcha"). Avoid over-articulation in natural conversation.
  • Vowel and Consonant Sounds – distinguish between minimal pairs like ship vs. sheep or bat vs. pat.

Nigerian Examples

  1. Example 1 (Intonation):
    "You are coming to the party?" (Rising intonation at the end indicates a question, even without auxiliary verb inversion).
    In contrast: "You are coming to the party." (Falling intonation indicates a statement.)
  2. Example 2 (Stress):
    In Nigerian English, the word development is often stressed on the second syllable: deVELopment (correct). Avoid stressing the first syllable: DEVelopment (incorrect).
Fun Fact 🎤
Did you know? In English, the sentence "I never said she stole my money" can have seven different meanings depending on which word you stress! Try stressing each word one at a time to see how the meaning changes.

Practice Questions

  1. Question 1: Which intonation pattern (rising or falling) would you use for the following question?
    "Are you going to the market?"
    Answer: Rising intonation (↗) – because it is a yes/no question.
  2. Question 2: Identify the stressed syllable in the word photography.
    Answer: The second syllable – phoTOGraphy.
  3. Question 3: Which word in this sentence should be stressed to emphasize that the action was not done by anyone else?
    "I baked the cake."
    Answer: Stress the word "I" – "I baked the cake."
