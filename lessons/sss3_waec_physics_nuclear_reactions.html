Nuclear Reactions

Subject: Physics (WAEC Syllabus) | Class: SSS3

Key Concepts

  • Nuclear Reaction: Process where two atomic nuclei or a nucleus and a subatomic particle collide, resulting in transformation of the nucleus.
  • Nuclear Fission: Splitting of a heavy nucleus (e.g., U-235) into two lighter nuclei, releasing neutrons and a large amount of energy (≈200 MeV per fission).
  • Chain Reaction: Released neutrons cause further fissions, leading to a self-sustaining reaction — controlled in reactors, uncontrolled in atomic bombs.
  • Nuclear Fusion: Combining two light nuclei (e.g., hydrogen isotopes) to form a heavier nucleus, releasing even more energy (powers the sun).
  • Mass-Energy Equivalence: Einstein’s \( E = mc^2 \) explains the energy released — a tiny mass defect converts to enormous energy.

🇳🇬 Nigerian Examples

1. Nigeria Research Reactor-1 (NIRR-1): Located at Ahmadu Bello University, Zaria. This miniature neutron source reactor uses nuclear fission of uranium-235 for research, neutron activation analysis, and production of radioisotopes for medicine and agriculture.

2. Gamma Irradiation Facility at Sheda Science & Technology Complex (SHESTCO): Uses cobalt-60 (produced via neutron capture in a nuclear reaction) to irradiate food for preservation and to sterilize medical equipment — reducing post-harvest losses in Nigeria.

⚡ Fun Fact! The Sun fuses about 600 million tons of hydrogen into helium every second, releasing energy equivalent to billions of nuclear bombs — yet only a tiny fraction reaches Earth, enough to power all life!

Practice Questions

  1. Explain the difference between nuclear fission and nuclear fusion. Give one application of each.
  2. In a nuclear fission chain reaction, what role do the released neutrons play? How is this controlled in a nuclear reactor?
  3. Using Einstein’s mass-energy equation, calculate the energy released if 0.1 kg of uranium undergoes fission, assuming 0.1% of its mass is converted to energy. (Speed of light \( c = 3 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s} \))

Answers

  1. Fission: splitting heavy nucleus (e.g., U-235) → used in nuclear power plants. Fusion: joining light nuclei (e.g., hydrogen) → used in hydrogen bombs and future clean energy.
  2. Released neutrons strike other U-235 nuclei, causing further fissions (chain reaction). In reactors, control rods (e.g., boron or cadmium) absorb excess neutrons to maintain a steady rate.
  3. Mass converted = 0.1% of 0.1 kg = 0.0001 kg. Energy \( E = mc^2 = 0.0001 \times (3 \times 10^8)^2 = 0.0001 \times 9 \times 10^{16} = 9 \times 10^{12} \text{ J}\).
